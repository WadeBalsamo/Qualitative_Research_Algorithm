VAMMR THEME FRAMEWORK
=====================

Vigilance | Avoidance | Mindfulness | Metacognition | Reappraisal

Five stages of contemplative transformation expressed in participant
language during Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement (MORE) therapy
for chronic pain.


PURPOSE OF THE FRAMEWORK
------------------------

The VAMMR framework operationalizes the developmental trajectory by which
patients with chronic pain restructure their relationship to pain through
mindfulness practice. It is designed to support per-utterance classification
of participant segments in qualitative analysis and computational mixed-
methods pipelines (the QRA system), enabling the empirical study of
therapeutic progression at corpus scale.

The framework captures five qualitatively distinct ways participants
express their engagement with pain in MORE sessions. The ordering — from
Vigilance through Reappraisal — reflects an increasing adaptiveness of
the relationship between attention and the lived body, from initial
attentional colonization by pain to the transformative reinterpretation
of pain experience. Participants do not progress through the stages
linearly. Instead, the stages describe a space of possible expressions
that participants move through fluidly, often within a single session,
with the modal stage advancing across the eight weeks of the program.


WHAT CHANGED FROM THE PRIOR FRAMEWORK
-------------------------------------

The original four-stage VA-MR framework, derived by Wexler, Balsamo et al.
(2026) from thematic analysis of MORE sessions for lumbosacral radicular
pain, identified four stages and one barrier: Pain Vigilance and Attention
Dysregulation, Competency with Attention Regulation, Metacognitive
Awareness, and Pain Reappraisal, with Experiential Avoidance treated as a
barrier within the Attention Regulation stage.

The current revision restructures the framework along three dimensions.

  1. Experiential Avoidance is promoted to its own stage rather than being
     treated as a barrier within attentional development. In the prior
     framework, "competency with attention regulation" and "experiential
     avoidance" were folded together inside a single stage, with avoidance
     described as a tendency that emerged within it. This conflation made
     the stage statistically heterogeneous and obscured an important
     developmental signal: the moment when emerging attentional skill stops
     being deployed for escape and starts being deployed for presence.
     Separating them yields a meaningful Avoidance to Mindfulness transition
     that is now empirically interrogable.

  2. Avoidance is defined narrowly. Rather than encompassing every instance
     of attention being directed somewhere other than pain, the construct
     now requires three diagnostic features: an escape goal, unstable
     attention that cannot remain with discomfort, and either escapist
     character (suppression, distraction, flying away) or kinesiophobic
     character (fear of movement). The narrower definition prevents
     Avoidance from absorbing legitimate adaptive coping and produces
     cleaner transition statistics in the cue-response analysis.

  3. Reappraisal is defined broadly. Rather than restricting the construct
     to sensory reinterpretation of pain, the revised definition encompasses
     both sensory reappraisal (decomposition into constituent sensations,
     jagged-to-dull transformations, sensation distinguished from suffering)
     and cognitive reappraisal (identity-level redefinition, worldview
     shifts, pain no longer defining the self). Equanimity and acceptance
     are explicitly included, but only when grounded in described insight
     rather than in suppression — this is the critical boundary against
     Avoidance.

The framework name changes from VA-MR to VAMMR to reflect the addition of
the Mindfulness stage. Stage 2's prior label "Attention Regulation and
Development of Present Awareness" has been simplified to "Mindfulness."
The shorter name aligns with established terminology in the contemplative
science literature and produces a cleaner mnemonic; the substantive content
of the stage is the development of mindfulness as an adaptive skill, which
is what the simpler label now expresses.


THE FIVE STAGES
---------------

VIGILANCE — Pain Vigilance and Catastrophizing

Hypervigilant monitoring of pain signals coupled with the absence of any
successful attentional deployment. The participant is captured or
overwhelmed by pain, attention scatters or fragments, and the participant
cannot sustain focus on chosen objects of awareness. Catastrophic
cognitions about pain may be present: pain framed as a life sentence,
endless, defining, or controlling. Restlessness during meditation and
the inability to remain still are common physical correlates. In
phenomenological terms this corresponds to Leder's (1990) dys-appearance
in its most acute form — the body insisting on appearing as obstacle
rather than receding as the transparent medium of intentional engagement.

The diagnostic feature is the absence of any successful attentional
strategy, adaptive or maladaptive. The participant has not yet developed
even a maladaptive redirection — pain captures awareness despite efforts
to engage with practice.

Representative quote from the MORE corpus:

  "...if I'm still, my body, the pain in the hips will flare up. So I'll
  have to move. And it's like a butterfly effect with my brain. Once I
  have that pain and I move, my focus is lost."


AVOIDANCE — Experiential Avoidance

A narrowly defined maladaptive deployment of emerging attentional skill
in the service of escape from internal experience. The participant has
acquired some attentional capacity but uses it to redirect AWAY from pain
rather than to remain WITH it. Two subtypes are included: escapist
avoidance (deliberately ignoring, suppressing, distracting from, or
"flying away from" pain sensations, thoughts, or emotions) and
kinesiophobic avoidance (fear-based avoidance of movement, posture, or
activity expected to provoke pain).

The diagnostic feature is unstable attention. The participant cannot
remain with discomfort and must redirect away. The goal is to NOT FEEL,
not to investigate. Relief, when reported, comes from suppression,
disconnection, or distraction rather than from understanding. Mindfulness
itself may be reframed as a tool for escape — described as a "superpower"
to fly somewhere else.

Representative quote from the MORE corpus:

  "It [mindfulness] becomes like a superpower... to fly you somewhere
  else away from your sorrows and away from the pain."

The narrowed definition is consequential for the transition analysis.
Many surface behaviors that look like avoidance — focusing on the breath,
relaxing during practice, attending to a savoring object — are NOT coded
Avoidance unless the diagnostic features are present. A participant who
focuses on the breath as an anchor for sustained presence with sensation
is engaged in Mindfulness. A participant who focuses on the breath to
escape sensation, with attention destabilizing around discomfort, is
engaged in Avoidance. The boundary is the participant's relationship to
present experience, not the surface object of attention.


MINDFULNESS — Sustained Attention and Present Awareness

The development of mindfulness as an adaptive skill: stable, sustained,
volitional attention that can stay WITH present experience including pain
or discomfort, without redirecting away. The participant describes
increasing capacity to enter and remain in present awareness, reduced
effortful struggle between attention and distraction, and the ability to
bring attention to chosen objects (breath, body, sensation) and hold it
there. Attention functions as anchor rather than vehicle for escape: the
participant can return to the breath after distraction without using the
breath to flee from sensation.

This is the foundational mindfulness skill underlying the more
sophisticated stages that follow. It is present awareness as it is —
without yet describing observation of mental processes themselves
(Metacognition) or transformation of pain experience (Reappraisal). Pain
may still be pain at this stage, but the participant can be present with
what is.

Representative quote from the MORE corpus:

  "There's less fight going on internally as far as the intention goes
  than it was four weeks ago. It's almost like a switch now. Whereas
  before, it was really back and forth, back and forth. And now it's
  operating much smoother."

In contemplative-tradition terms, the Mindfulness stage corresponds most
closely to what is sometimes called calm abiding or shamatha — the
foundational stabilization of attention on which more advanced
contemplative skills are built. The framework does not assume Western
participants in eight weeks of clinical MBI access the depths of these
practices documented in long-term contemplatives, but the developmental
ordering — sustained presence as the precondition for reflexive observation
and transformative reinterpretation — is preserved.


METACOGNITION — Metacognitive Awareness

The reflexive capacity to observe one's own mental processes as they
occur. The participant describes noticing reactions to pain, watching
thoughts and impulses arise and pass, recognizing characteristic patterns
in their own responses, and adopting an observing perspective from which
mental activity can be examined rather than identified with. This is
decentering: recognizing that one is HAVING a thought rather than BEING
the thought; recognizing the difference between pain and the reaction to
pain; recognizing that anticipation can amplify pain before the sensation
even arrives.

Metacognition presupposes the attentional stability of Mindfulness but
adds a reflexive observing position. It does not yet involve described
transformation of the experience itself. The participant observes their
experience rather than reinterpreting it; pain may be observed as still
being pain, but observed FROM somewhere rather than FROM WITHIN.

Representative quote from the MORE corpus:

  "I caught myself at one point during the body scan when you were going
  down the body... I noticed tension right here as you were approaching
  that... and I was like, that's anticipation. My brain is already
  deciding that it's kind of hurt before I even get there. So I actually
  tried to relax right here because I felt myself doing that."


REAPPRAISAL — Cognitive and Sensory Reappraisal

A transformation of the meaning, structure, or felt quality of pain
experience that emerges from insight rather than from suppression. Two
complementary domains are encompassed.

SENSORY REAPPRAISAL: pain decomposed into constituent sensations (heat,
tingling, pressure; jagged versus dull; sharp versus soft; hot versus
cool), described as changing or impermanent rather than monolithic, with
sensation explicitly distinguished from suffering. The felt quality of
pain shifts as the participant attends to it directly. This is the
mechanism of mindful reappraisal of pain sensations as theorized in the
MORE model and operationalized in the Mindful Reappraisal of Pain
Sensations Scale.

COGNITIVE REAPPRAISAL: pain redefined in terms of identity, life
trajectory, or self-narrative; pain no longer defining the self; new
sense of freedom, possibility, or worldview; pain reframed as a challenge
rather than a struggle. This includes equanimity and acceptance that
emerge from described understanding rather than from effortful
suppression.

The critical boundary against Avoidance is the requirement of described
insight. When participants report that pain bothers them less or has
shifted, the report is coded Reappraisal ONLY when grounded in described
insight, transformed perception, or sensory decomposition — NOT in
suppression, distraction, or simple absence of feeling.

Representative quotes from the MORE corpus:

  Sensory:
  "When I went into the pain part of it, it was very jagged and sharp
  and hot and red... and then, as I breathed more into it, it got less
  sharp, more of a dull. And the color changed for me from a red to a
  dark blue."

  Cognitive:
  "I have to redefine my definition of pain... that's kind of what I've
  been set out to do is to redefine my definition of pain and not let
  it define me and control me and keep me just useless."


BOUNDARY CASES BETWEEN ADJACENT STAGES
--------------------------------------

The most consequential discriminations for accurate classification are
between adjacent stages, where surface features may converge but the
underlying structure differs. Each pair below is the boundary that the
adversarial utterance set in the codebook is designed to test.

VIGILANCE versus AVOIDANCE

Both involve pain occupying attention, but Vigilance is capture without
successful redirection, while Avoidance is enacted redirection that
suppresses experience. The distinguishing question: has the participant
established a deliberate strategy of redirection, however unsustainable?
"I try to push the pain away but it keeps grabbing my attention" is
Vigilance when the capture dominates the report; Avoidance when the
effortful redirection is the focus.

AVOIDANCE versus MINDFULNESS

The most clinically important boundary in the framework. Both can involve
"focusing on the breath," but with opposite intent. Avoidance uses the
breath as a vehicle for escape; Mindfulness uses the breath as an anchor
for staying with experience. The diagnostic feature is whether attention
remains with experience (including discomfort) or destabilizes around it.
"I focused on the breath and the pain felt less important" requires
contextual interpretation: Avoidance if the breath is escape and attention
flees from sensation, Mindfulness if the breath is anchor and the
participant describes staying with what is.

MINDFULNESS versus METACOGNITION

Sustained presence with experience versus presence plus observation of
mental processes. Mindfulness is attention WITH content; Metacognition
adds explicit observation of mental activity itself. "I noticed I could
keep my attention where I wanted it" is Mindfulness when emphasis is on
sustained control; Metacognition when emphasis is on the observing
perspective itself.

METACOGNITION versus REAPPRAISAL

Observation versus transformation. Both involve a relationship to one's
experience that includes some distance, but Metacognition stays at the
level of observation while Reappraisal describes the experience itself
as transformed. "I noticed the pain was changing" is Metacognition when
the report is observational; Reappraisal when the change is described as
a transformation in what pain IS for the participant — sensory
decomposition, lost solidity, shifted meaning.

REAPPRAISAL versus AVOIDANCE

The boundary against which Reappraisal must be defined most carefully.
Both can produce reports that pain bothers the participant less. The
discriminating feature is described insight. "It doesn't bother me
anymore" without described insight is Avoidance; with described insight
or transformed perception, it is Reappraisal. Equanimity grounded in
understanding is Reappraisal; equanimity as disengagement strategy is
Avoidance.


WORD PROTOTYPES
---------------

Each stage in the codebook includes a substantially expanded set of
word prototypes — the characteristic lexical register of the stage.
These prototypes are not classification rules; the LLM classifier
considers the full context of an utterance in light of the stage
definition, prototypical features, distinguishing criteria, and exemplar
utterances. The word prototypes serve as the embedding space landmarks
that orient the classifier toward each stage's characteristic vocabulary.

Word prototypes are derived from MORE session transcripts and capture
the actual language participants use rather than synthetic terminology.
For example, Vigilance prototypes include "butterfly effect" (a
participant's metaphor for attentional disruption following pain
movement) and "attention span of a squirrel" (a participant's
self-description of dysregulated attention). Mindfulness prototypes
include "operating much smoother" and "less fight going on internally"
(participant descriptions of attentional development across weeks).
Reappraisal prototypes include "jagged to dull," "crisp sheets," and
"new lease on life" (participant phrases capturing sensory and cognitive
transformation).


NOTES ON USE
------------

The codebook is intended as input to LLM-based classification of
participant segments and as reference material for human qualitative
coders working in parallel. The exemplar, subtle, and adversarial
utterances together form a content-validity test set: the classifier
should correctly handle exemplars (clear cases), should achieve
reasonable performance on subtle cases, and should be tested explicitly
on adversarial cases (boundary discriminations). When the classifier
fails on adversarial items, the framework definitions or the prompt
should be refined before committing compute to the full corpus.

Participants move between stages fluidly. A given session may contain
expressions of all five stages within a single participant's contributions,
with the modal stage advancing across the eight-week program. Therapeutic
progression is not interpreted as movement of a single trajectory point
but as a shift in the distribution of participant expression toward later
stages over the course of the intervention.

The framework does not interpret stage occupancy as evaluative judgment
of the participant. Vigilance is not failure; it is the starting condition
that the intervention is designed to address. Avoidance is not character
weakness; it is a developmentally common waypoint. Mindfulness is not
the endpoint; it is a foundational skill on which the more transformative
stages depend. The framework characterizes a developmental space, not
a hierarchy of merit.
