# Phenomenology at Trial Speed: A Computational Mixed-Methods Pipeline for Iterative Refinement of Mindfulness-Movement Therapy in Chronic Pain

**Wade Balsamo, Ryan S. Wexler ND MSCR, and the Move-MORE Research Team at NUNM**

*In preparation for submission to a mixed-methods methodology venue (Journal of Mixed Methods Research, BMC Medical Research Methodology, or International Journal of Qualitative Methods).*

Running head: *Phenomenology at Trial Speed*

---

## Abstract

Iterative early-phase clinical trials face a structural mismatch between the temporal demands of careful qualitative analysis and the brevity of between-cohort refinement windows. The mismatch is most consequential for phenomenologically-grounded interventions like mindfulness-based therapy for chronic pain, where the active ingredients of treatment are first-person experiential transformations that conventional outcome measures cannot fully capture. We describe the Qualitative Research Algorithm (QRA), a computational pipeline that operationalizes two complementary phenomenological frameworks against the diarized session transcripts of a feasibility trial — the Vigilance–Avoidance–Attention Regulation–Metacognition–Reappraisal (VAAMR) model of therapeutic progression in Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement (Wexler, Balsamo et al., 2026), and the 59-code Varieties of Contemplative Experience (VCE) phenomenology codebook of Lindahl et al. (2017). QRA classifies every participant segment against VAAMR through multi-run large-language-model consensus voting, applies multi-label VCE coding through an embedding-plus-LLM ensemble, computes empirical (stage × code) lift statistics, and indexes therapist segments for cue-response analysis at every observed stage transition.

We argue that the pipeline *scaffolds the conditions for* a Varela-style (1996) neurophenomenological mutual-constraints test, rather than operationalizing one in a single pass. The four-cohort iterative design is integral to this argument: lift patterns observed in Cohorts 1–2 generate theoretically-interpretable expectations that — once encoded as a data structure in `theme_framework/vaamr.py` before Cohort 3 begins — become the formal pre-registered hypothesis tested by Cohorts 3–4 as replication. We are explicit about what the pipeline currently computes (exploratory lift characterization, no automated expected-vs-observed comparison) and what is committed for the between-cohort window before Cohort 3.

The methodology's primary pilot deployment is the Move-MORE Feasibility Trial — a four-cohort adaptation of MORE integrating movement-based therapy for adults with lumbosacral radicular pain and elevated kinesiophobia. As of this writing, Cohorts 1 and 2 have completed and four qualitative researchers are validating a 20% stratified sample of QRA's VAAMR classifications. We describe what the pipeline reveals that conventional methods cannot, the curriculum-modification workflow it supports, the Text Psychometrics validation framework (Low et al., 2024) it implements, and the principled extensions — most consequentially the PURER classification of therapist dialogue — that will complete the dyadic structure of the analysis before the trial's final cohort.

**Keywords:** computational phenomenology; mixed methods; iterative trial design; chronic pain; mindfulness-based interventions; lived body; Vigilance-Avoidance-Attention Regulation-Metacognition-Reappraisal; Varieties of Contemplative Experience; large language models; text psychometrics

## 1. The Methodological Problem

Iterative clinical trial designs share a structural feature with significant practical consequences. Each cohort's experience is intended to inform the next cohort's curriculum, and the analytic synthesis that should drive between-cohort modification has to be completed within weeks, sometimes days. For interventions whose active mechanisms are quantitatively measurable — symptom reductions, biomarker changes, behavioral counts — this temporal demand is reasonable; modern outcome dashboards generate the relevant evidence quickly. But for interventions whose active mechanisms are first-person experiential transformations, the temporal demand collides with the time qualitative analysis genuinely requires.

Mindfulness-based interventions for chronic pain are the case in point. The phenomenological frame most fully developed by Leder (1990) building on Merleau-Ponty (1962) describes chronic pain not as a persistent unpleasant sensation but as a structural disorder of embodied experience — the body's pathological insistence on appearing as obstacle rather than receding as the transparent medium of intentional engagement. Mindfulness training, on this account, is a structured practice of phenomenological re-habituation: it does not seek to make pain disappear but to alter the structural relationship between attention and the painful body such that somatic signals can be registered without organizing the entire field of experience around themselves. The empirical evidence that Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement (MORE; Garland, 2024) produces clinically meaningful effects is substantial (Garland et al., 2022), and Wexler, Balsamo et al. (2026) recently characterized the five-stage phenomenological progression — Vigilance, Avoidance, Attention Regulation, Metacognition, Reappraisal — by which participants traverse this re-habituation in MORE for lumbosacral radicular pain.

But that characterization required months of careful thematic analysis on thirty session recordings — the time genuine qualitative attentiveness demands. The Move-MORE Feasibility Trial that builds on this work runs four cohorts in a learning-health-system design with between-cohort windows measured in weeks. Without a tractable methodology for systematic phenomenological analysis at trial speed, between-cohort refinements default to clinical intuition and aggregate outcome scores — important inputs, but ones that cannot answer the questions a phenomenologically-grounded intervention most needs to answer about itself.

The Qualitative Research Algorithm (QRA) was built to dissolve this tension. It does not replace human qualitative analysis; it deploys human qualitative attention efficiently — labeling at corpus scale where labeling is tractable, surfacing the cases where labeling is ambiguous, and organizing the dyadic structure of session interaction so that the relationship between therapist behavior and participant phenomenological progression becomes empirically interrogable within the temporal constraints of iterative design.

This paper has three aims. First, to document what the pipeline currently computes and what it does not, with explicit identification of every place the manuscript prose describes implementation that exists versus engineering committed for the between-cohort window. Second, to articulate what this methodology uniquely reveals — the per-utterance phenomenological staging at corpus scale, the temporally-adjacent mechanism evidence, the dyadic structure derived from a single input, the empirical evaluation of cross-framework convergent expectations, and the validated labeled corpus produced as cumulative byproduct. Third, to describe how the Move-MORE Feasibility Trial — currently completing its second of four cohorts, with human validation underway — serves as the methodology's primary pilot deployment.

## 2. Theoretical Grounding: The Lived Body and Its Re-habituation

### 2.1 Chronic Pain as Dys-appearance

Merleau-Ponty's (1962) account of the lived body distinguishes the body-as-experienced from the body-as-physiological-object. In fluid embodiment, the body recedes from focal attention precisely because it is functioning as the condition of attention itself — the medium through which a perceiver reaches toward the world. The skilled typist, the practiced violinist, the walking pedestrian: in each case the body that serves as the instrument of skilled action is characteristically absent from focal attention because it is functioning well. The body is most fully present as lived body when it is most fully transparent as object.

Leder (1990) extends this analysis to the body's pathological modes of appearance. In *dys-appearance*, the body erupts into thematic consciousness as alien — as that which resists the subject's projects and demands attention that would otherwise be directed outward. The dys-appearing body does not disappear into its functions; it inserts itself between subject and world, reconfiguring the intentional arc. Chronic pain produces dys-appearance as a sustained structural condition: the patient inhabits a body that continuously demands attentional resources, narrowing the perceptual horizon, foreclosing engaged action, and reorganizing self-experience around the figure of the painful body. Pain catastrophizing, hypervigilant body monitoring, and the affective constriction documented in chronic pain populations are not separate phenomena from pain itself but constitutive features of the dys-appearance state — the structural form pain's pathological insistence takes when consciousness is colonized by its signal.

This phenomenological account complements rather than contradicts neuroscientific descriptions of chronic pain. Where neuroscience describes the mechanism (central sensitization, default-mode-network hyperconnectivity, descending modulation dysfunction), phenomenology describes the experiential structure that the mechanism produces and that, in turn, sustains it. The two descriptions converge on the same therapeutic target — the disrupted relation between attention and the body — approached from opposite epistemic directions.

### 2.2 The VAAMR Stages as Trajectory of Re-habituation

The Vigilance-Avoidance-Attention Regulation-Metacognition-Reappraisal model emerged from thematic analysis of thirty MORE sessions in the parent trial for patients with lumbosacral radicular pain (Wexler, Balsamo et al., 2026). What is theoretically significant about the framework, beyond the five qualitatively distinct patterns of in-session expression it describes, is that those patterns can be interpreted as stages in the progressive restoration of healthy structural relationships between attention and the lived body — the very process Section 2.1's phenomenological analysis describes.

**Stage 0 — Pain Vigilance and Attention Dysregulation.** Attentional colonization by pain. Canonical expression: *"I can't stop thinking about the pain, it's all I can focus on."* Phenomenologically, this is Leder's dys-appearance at its most acute: the body occupying the total field of attentional engagement, leaving no ground from which observation or deliberate action is possible. The subject is not, at this moment, a perspective on pain; she is, in this moment, constituted by it.

**Stage 1 — Attention Regulation Applied to Experiential Avoidance.** The structurally important transition that the Wexler et al. (2026) analysis identified as the central developmental barrier of MORE — and the transition most often misread as progress when it is in fact an obstacle to it. Canonical expression: *"When the pain comes, I focus really hard on my breathing to push it away."* Phenomenologically closer to Heidegger's (1962) present-at-hand orientation — the body as object to be managed — than to the restored transparency of the ready-to-hand. The participant has acquired attentional skill but deploys it *away from* somatic experience rather than *toward* it. Hayes et al.'s (1996) experiential-avoidance construct captures the psychological dynamics; the phenomenological reading adds that this avoidance maintains the structural disruption chronic pain produced even as it provides symptomatic relief.

**Stage 2 — Attention Regulation.** Stable, sustained, volitional attention that stays *with* present experience — including somatic sensation — rather than directing attention away from it or being captured by it. Canonical expression: *"I kept bringing my attention back to the sensations, just staying with them."* Phenomenologically, this is the emergence of attentional skill deployed toward experience rather than away from it: the participant has acquired the capacity that Avoidance deployed defensively and now applies it with investigative intent. The mindful attention does not fight the sensation or observe it from detached distance; it rests in it as in a stable field. Distinguishing criterion from Avoidance: the attentional direction is toward rather than away; distinguishing criterion from Metacognition: the attention is anchored in present-moment somatic experience rather than reflexively observing itself observing.

**Stage 3 — Metacognitive Awareness.** The emergence of *reflexive distance*: the capacity to observe one's own mental processes without immediately identifying with them. Canonical expression: *"I noticed I was getting anxious about the pain, and I could just watch that anxiety."* Husserl (1983) describes this in terms of the act/object distinction — metacognition takes one's own intentional acts (anxiety, catastrophizing, avoidance-impulses) as intentional objects, converting them from unexamined conditions of experience into examined contents of attention. Hanley et al. (2020) operationalize this as decentering through the Metacognitive Processes of Decentering Scale.

**Stage 4 — Pain Reappraisal.** A transformation of the noematic structure of pain experience: a change not merely in how pain is *related to* (as in metacognition) but in what pain itself is *experienced as*. Canonical expression: *"It's interesting, when I really look at it, the 'pain' is actually many different feelings."* Pain decomposed into constituent sensations loses its character as a monolithic threatening event and becomes a complex, textured, changing field. The Mindful Reappraisal of Pain Scale (Garland et al., 2022) operationalizes this empirically; correlations with neural metrics from Riegner et al. (2023) and Zeidan et al. (2015) suggest that this reappraisal corresponds to genuine changes in pain processing architecture, not merely linguistic reframing.

The arc from Stage 0 to Stage 4 describes the progressive recovery of the body as a transparent medium of experience rather than a dysappearing obstacle: from attentional colonization (Vigilance) through instrumental management (Avoidance) to sustained open attention (Attention Regulation), observing perspective (Metacognition), and finally to noematic transformation (Reappraisal).

### 2.3 PURER as Structured Phenomenological Inquiry

Each MORE session follows a structured therapist inquiry format: **P**henomenology, **U**tilization, **R**eframing, **E**ducation/Expectancy, **R**einforcement. PURER functions as something analogous to what Giorgi (1985) describes as the descriptive phenomenological interview: a structured method for eliciting, exploring, and consolidating participants' first-person reports.

The Phenomenology component — *"What did you notice during the practice?"* — is most directly analogous to Husserlian *epoché*: the invitation to bracket ordinary naturalistic assumptions and describe experience as it presents itself. Reframing offers interpretive proposals — *"Can you let yourself just notice the sensations without calling them pain?"* — that function as Giorgi's free imaginative variation. Education/Expectancy provides conceptual scaffolding; Reinforcement consolidates and amplifies expressions of therapeutic insight. Utilization bridges in-session insight to ongoing pain-management challenges.

The methodological consequence: therapist dialogue in MORE is not merely contextual background to participant phenomenological expression but is itself a *systematic elicitor* of phenomenological description. The structure of PURER shapes what kinds of phenomenological reports participants offer. QRA captures this shaping empirically through the PURER classification implemented at Stage 3c of the pipeline (Section 4.8), which classifies therapist segments at the cue-block level — the therapist's full response between consecutive participant turns — against the five PURER constructs. This bilateral classification is now operational on Cohorts 1–2; human validation of PURER labels is underway (Section 8.1).

## 3. The Dual-Framework Architecture

QRA implements two complementary phenomenological frameworks concurrently on the same transcript corpus. They address orthogonal analytical questions, and their concurrent application generates evidence neither framework can produce alone.

### 3.1 VAAMR: Where Is This Participant in Therapeutic Progression?

The VAAMR framework characterizes the developmental trajectory described in Section 2.2. Operationally, it is implemented in `theme_framework/vaamr.py` as a `ThemeFramework` containing five `ThemeDefinition` objects, one per stage. Each definition encodes a complete operational specification for LLM-based classification: a formal definition, twelve prototypical linguistic features, distinguishing criteria specifying how to discriminate the stage from its most easily confused neighbors, four exemplar utterances (clear expressions), three subtle utterances (harder to classify), three adversarial utterances (potentially confusable with adjacent stages), and a small set of word prototypes that capture each stage's characteristic lexical register.

The adversarial utterances are particularly important. They encode the conceptual boundaries between stages — the cases where clinical understanding is most needed and where automated classification is most challenged. *"I try to push the pain away but it keeps grabbing my attention"* sits at the Vigilance–Avoidance boundary: it might be Avoidance (if the *attempt* is the relevant feature) or Vigilance (if the *capture* is the relevant feature). *"I kept bringing my attention back to the breath"* sits at the Avoidance–Attention Regulation boundary: Avoidance if the breath is used to suppress pain sensation, Attention Regulation if the participant is sustaining open investigative presence. *"I noticed myself wanting to push the pain away and I just let that happen"* sits at the Attention Regulation–Metacognition boundary. These adversarial utterances surface important classification boundaries. Surfacing them as a known-label test set before processing real transcripts (Section 5.1) makes operational ambiguity explicit and testable rather than implicit in classifier behavior.

### 3.2 VCE: What Are Participants Phenomenologically Experiencing?

The Varieties of Contemplative Experience codebook (Lindahl et al., 2017) emerged from content-driven thematic analysis of 60 structured interviews with Western Buddhist meditators across Theravāda, Zen, and Tibetan traditions, deliberately including practitioners who reported meditation-related challenges as well as those reporting positive outcomes. It identifies 59 categories of contemplative experience organized across seven higher-order domains: Affective (13 codes), Cognitive (10 codes), Perceptual (7 codes), Conative (3 codes), Sense of Self (6 codes), Social (5 codes), and Somatic (15 codes).

Three features make the VCE taxonomy particularly suited as a parallel classification framework for MBI research. First, it is grounded in careful first-person phenomenological reporting across multiple traditions. Second, it is explicitly designed to be domain-descriptive rather than valence-prescriptive: it documents the phenomenological content of contemplative experience without classifying experiences as inherently beneficial or adverse, recognizing that the same phenomenological category may be experienced as transformative, threatening, or neutral depending on context, framework, and support. Third, its 59-code resolution enables fine-grained characterization of experiential content that coarser-grained instruments cannot.

The relationship between VCE's tradition-informed taxonomy and the clinical MBI context requires explicit acknowledgment. VCE was derived from practitioners for whom meditation is embedded in a comprehensive practice context — ethical training, communal support, tradition-specific interpretive frameworks, soteriological goals well beyond chronic pain management. Move-MORE participants engage in brief mindfulness practices in a medically framed group context; the phenomenological depths accessible in eight weeks of clinical MBI practice differ significantly from those documented in long-term contemplatives. The pipeline makes no assumption about which codes should be common; the empirical cross-validation is designed to reveal this directly.

### 3.3 Why Both — and a Prominent Disclosure About What the Code Currently Does

The frameworks are orthogonal in their analytical contributions. VAAMR is a developmental stage model: it asks *where* in the trajectory of contemplative transformation a given utterance is located. VCE is a content taxonomy: it asks *what* the participant is phenomenologically experiencing. Their concurrent application enables cross-framework empirical evidence neither alone could produce.

Before stating the theoretically motivated co-occurrence expectations, we make a disclosure that the rest of this section's argument depends on:

> **The cross-validation as currently implemented is purely exploratory.** The codebase computes empirical (VAAMR stage × VCE code) lift statistics filtered by lift ≥ 1.5 and minimum count ≥ 3 (`process/cross_validation.py`); it does *not* compare those statistics against pre-specified expected pairs encoded in any data structure. The expectations stated below are stated in this manuscript prose, not as `expected_codes` in `theme_framework/vaamr.py`. A reader cannot verify from the codebase alone which (stage, code) pairs were anticipated before the lift table was inspected. This is consequential and we describe its remedy below before stating the expectations.

With that disclosure on the table, the phenomenological theory developed in Section 2 motivates a set of theoretically grounded expectations:

| VAAMR stage | Theoretically expected to show elevated lift in | Theoretical rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Vigilance | Fear/Anxiety/Panic; Agitation or Irritability; Pain (Somatic) | Dys-appearance as affective-somatic phenomenon characterized by hypervigilant monitoring and anxious anticipation |
| Avoidance | Affective Flattening; Emotional Detachment/Alexithymia | Emotional blunting documented in experiential-avoidance strategies (Hayes et al., 1996) — the motivated effort to *not experience* pain-related affect produces broader affective constriction |
| Attention Regulation | Attention; Change in Duration of Experience; Somatic Relaxation or Calming | Sustained volitional attention to somatic experience without avoidance or evaluative elaboration; characteristic shift in temporal quality of experience; body settling as attention anchors rather than fights sensation |
| Metacognition | Meta-Cognition (Cognitive); Clarity; Change in Narrative Self | Emergence of reflexive self-observation; beginning of narrative distance from pain-defined self-identity |
| Reappraisal | Positive Affect; Change in Worldview; Change in Self-Other or Self-World Boundaries; Disintegration of Conceptual Meaning Structures | Noematic transformation of pain experience with attendant changes in sense of self and world |

**The staged operationalization of mutual constraints.** Three points warrant emphasis about how the relationship between these expectations and the iterative cohort design transforms the manuscript's epistemological claim.

*First*, the Stage 4 lift computation (Section 4.5) on Cohorts 1–2 generates the empirical co-occurrence patterns against which these expectations *can be evaluated by inspection*. Whether Cohorts 1–2 confirm or disconfirm them is determinable from the lift table; the pre-specification of expectations, however, lives in this manuscript, not in the running code.

*Second*, this is precisely the situation the iterative cohort design is well-positioned to address. The empirical patterns observed in Cohorts 1–2 — both confirmations of the theoretical predictions above and unanticipated high-lift associations — generate a refined and code-level-specifiable set of expectations that can be pre-registered before Cohort 3 begins as formal hypotheses for replication in Cohorts 3–4. *The iterative cohort design is the mutual-constraints architecture.* Cohorts 1–2 generate empirical regularities under theoretically-motivated description; Cohorts 3–4 test whether those regularities replicate against pre-specified, code-encoded expectations.

*Third*, the structural commitment to add an `expected_codes` field to `ThemeDefinition` in `theme_schema.py`, populated in `vaamr.py` with the predictions from the table above, before Cohort 3 begins, is recorded as the highest-priority engineering item for the between-cohort interval (Section 8.2). Once that field is populated, the cross-validation report mechanically generates expected-vs-observed comparisons (predicted-and-confirmed, predicted-and-absent, unpredicted-but-elevated) rather than the prose-level evaluation that is currently possible. This addresses the strongest available critique of the present analysis — that without code-level pre-specification, predictions cannot be verified as having been stated before the data that would test them — by committing to that specification publicly before the data are generated.

The manuscript's epistemological claim is therefore that QRA *scaffolds the conditions for* a Varela-style (1996) mutual-constraints test, not that it operationalizes one in a single pass. The scaffolding includes the corpus, the dual-framework infrastructure, the lift table, the pre-specification commitment, and the replication architecture provided by the four-cohort iterative design. To our knowledge, this constitutes the first systematic empirical examination of the phenomenological correlates of VAAMR stage expression and the first application of the VCE taxonomy to a chronic pain MBI population.

### 3.4 The Hypotheses, Stated as Falsifiable Claims

The preceding sections motivate a set of expectations in prose. Good scientific practice requires that those expectations be stated as explicit, falsifiable hypotheses, each paired with the measurement that tests it, the direction that would confirm it, and the result that would refute it. We consolidate them here. Hypotheses H1–H2 concern the substantive clinical phenomenon; H3 and its sub-hypothesis H3a concern the dual-framework architecture and are the focus of this section's methodological contribution; H4–H5 concern external and computational validity; H6 concerns the discriminant validity of the VAAMR construct and was *surfaced by the Cohorts 1–2 pilot* rather than pre-specified. Each is *directional until human validation and (where applicable) the pre-registration commitments of Section 8.2 are in place* — Cohorts 1–2 estimate, Cohorts 3–4 confirm. Where the pilot has already produced a directional result, it is reported inline as a "pilot result/status" — most consequentially, **H5 (graph-as-scaler) is refuted at the pilot's scale and H2 (mechanism) is under-identified**, which together motivate repositioning the graph from a classifier to a mechanism instrument (Section 8.5).

**H1 — Developmental progression.** Over the eight-session program, participant language advances along the VA→MR arc: the group-mean continuous progression coordinate (E[stage], `analysis/efficacy.py`) has a positive slope across sessions, and the Avoidance→Attention-Regulation transition (the avoidance barrier) is the rate-limiting step. *Confirmed by* a positive group slope with a cluster-bootstrap CI excluding zero and a measurable barrier-crossing rate; *refuted by* a flat/negative slope or no identifiable barrier. *Instrument:* program-efficacy dossier + mechanism barrier analysis.

**H2 — Context-dependent therapist effects (mechanism).** Specific PURER moves and GNN-discovered cue motifs are associated with forward VAAMR transitions, and that association is *moderated by the participant's FROM stage* (Section 7.6) — the same move helps more at some stages than others. *Confirmed by* from-stage-conditioned Δprogression effects surviving FDR correction and a within-stratum permutation null; *refuted by* null conditional effects or effects that vanish under permutation. *Instrument:* `analysis/mechanism.py` (Δprogression table, mixed-effects model, permutation control). **Pilot status (n≈32): under-identified, not adjudicable.** No (from_stage × move) cell survives FDR at this scale (the observed table has *zero* FDR-significant cells), and the rebuilt dyadic FROM→CUE→TO transition model (Section 8.5, Track B) cannot show the therapist cue improving held-out prediction of the next participant state over a FROM-only baseline — the cue does not *earn its place* under participant-grouped cross-validation, so the transition is *under-identified* at this scale. (Its learned counterfactual does triangulate *positively* with the observed ranking, Spearman ρ ≈ +0.34 — a marked turn from the first, mis-specified per-segment-classifier counterfactual, which inverted the ranking at ρ = −0.13 (`GNN_MASTER_PLAN.md` §4.7) — but with zero FDR-significant observed cells the convergence is under-powered; coupling factors remain weak, |r| < 0.07.) The residual elicitation/responsiveness confound (Section 9.4) is *localized rather than dismissed*: therapists select moves in response to participant state, and the confound-localization map flags the (from_stage × move) cells where observed and counterfactual signals most diverge. H2 is therefore neither confirmed nor refuted by the pilot; it awaits the statistical power of Cohorts 3–4, and any positive result must then be read against this now-localized confound. The mechanism instruments (`mechanism.py` observed Δprogression; the transition model's counterfactual + its confound-localization map) are in place and validated; the data scale is the binding constraint.

**H3 — Cross-framework construct validity (mutual constraints).** The empirical (VAAMR stage × VCE code) lift table matches the theoretically pre-specified (stage, code) pairs of the Section 3.3 table at lift ≥ 1.5, *beyond what classifier co-dependency alone would produce*. *Confirmed by* predicted pairs showing elevated lift in the real assignment that does **not** survive the shuffled-stage permutation control (i.e., real ≫ permuted); *refuted by* predicted pairs absent in the real table, or present equally under permutation (construct overlap, not phenomenological structure — Section 5.2). *Instrument:* `process/cross_validation.py` + the `expected_codes` pre-specification and permutation control committed in Section 8.2.

**H3a — The VCE-on-VAAMR sub-hypothesis (does the granular codebook sharpen the broader themes?).** This is the question that justifies keeping VCE in the pipeline at all, stated as a computational hypothesis the graph can decide directly:

> *Adding the fine-grained 59-code VCE phenomenology codebook as a supervised layer **on top of** the coarse five-stage VAAMR arc improves the graph's ability to classify VAAMR — i.e., learning **what** a participant is experiencing sharpens the model's estimate of **where** they are in the developmental trajectory.*

The rationale is that the broad VAAMR themes of mindfulness-skill development are constituted by finer phenomenological content (Vigilance *is* a texture of fear/agitation/pain-monitoring; Reappraisal *is* a texture of positive-affect and worldview change — the Section 3.3 table). If those textures carry real signal, a model forced to predict them should organize its shared representation in a way that also discriminates the stages better. *Operationalization:* on the identical graph, folds, and seed, the held-out reliability cross-validation (Section 5.3) is run twice — with the `vce_multilabel` head active and with it removed — and the out-of-sample VAAMR κ vs the LLM consensus is compared, overall and per stage (`gnn_layer/ablation.py:vce_vaamr_contribution`, written to `06_gnn/vce_contribution.txt`). *Confirmed by* Δκ = κ(with VCE) − κ(without VCE) > 0, especially on the stages whose phenomenological content is most distinctive; *refuted by* Δκ ≈ 0 (VCE adds nothing the VAAMR signal lacks) or Δκ < 0 (VCE supervision distracts the shared representation). H3a is deliberately two-sided: a null or negative result is an informative, publishable finding, not a failure. **Pilot caveat (n≈32):** the operationalization above measures a Δκ on the *graph's* VAAMR classification, which the pilot shows is weak — the content-similarity graph does not recover the developmental label (Section 8.5), so a Δκ computed on a base classifier that does not earn its place is a low-power test of the substantive question. The substantive H3a question (does fine-grained phenomenological content sharpen the coarse stage estimate?) is retained, but should be re-instrumented on a classifier whose VAAMR reliability is established — the LLM consensus or the experimental Qwen probe — rather than on the graph, or deferred to the larger-N cohorts.

**Why VCE remains in the codebase — and the condition under which it would be retired.** VCE is *optional* (`run_codebook_classifier`, off in the minimal configuration) and applies only to participant segments; it is never a label of record and never crosses into VAAMR/PURER classification. Its continued presence is therefore not an assumption but a *standing, testable claim*: VCE earns its place only insofar as it (i) supplies construct-validity triangulation under H3 — independent convergent evidence that VAAMR stages have the phenomenological texture the theory predicts — and/or (ii) demonstrably improves the analysis under H3a. The pipeline now contains the instruments to adjudicate both (the permutation-controlled lift table for H3; `vce_vaamr_contribution` and the Capability-D head ablation for H3a). If, across the validated four-cohort corpus, VCE shows neither convergent lift beyond permutation (H3 refuted) nor a positive Δκ (H3a refuted), the principled conclusion is to retire it from the pipeline and report that the coarse VAAMR arc is sufficient — a decision we commit to making on the evidence rather than on attachment to the framework. Keeping VCE in the codebase is justified precisely because we have built the means to decide, empirically, whether it should stay.

**H4 — Convergent (criterion) validity against clinical outcomes.** Participants whose coded-language VAAMR trajectory advances more also improve more on independently measured clinical outcomes (pain NRS, TSK-11 kinesiophobia, ODI disability, MRPS reappraisal, MAIA-2 interoception). *Confirmed by* rank correlations in the pre-registered directions (Section 8.3) with effect sizes flagged for the small single-arm n; *refuted by* null or wrong-signed correlations. This is convergent-validity evidence that the language index tracks something clinically real — **not** efficacy, which the single-arm feasibility design cannot support. *Instrument:* `analysis/efficacy.py:link_to_external` once REDCap outcomes are integrated.

**H5 — Distillation and scalability.** A graph trained on the multi-run LLM consensus (itself bootstrapped by the human-validated subset) can reproduce that consensus to inter-rater reliability on held-out segments, enabling LLM-free classification of new cohorts. *Confirmed by* out-of-sample overall κ ≥ `irr_target` (0.70) with no rare-stage (Reappraisal) collapse; *refuted by* sub-threshold κ or per-stage collapse. *Instrument:* the reliability gate, `gnn_layer/validation.py` → `06_reports/06_gnn/validation.txt` (Section 5.3). **Pilot result (Cohorts 1–2, n≈32): refuted at this scale.** Under leakage-free participant-grouped cross-validation the graph reproduces the consensus on held-out *participants* at κ ≈ 0.05–0.14 — far below 0.70 and below the human↔human band — and a linear probe on the same Qwen features does not close the gap (Section 8.5). The graph is accordingly repositioned away from the classifier/scaler role; the LLM consensus, already human-level (κ = 0.537 vs human, Section 5.4), remains the scaling engine. The earlier κ ≈ 0.25 that appeared closer to the gate was an artifact of random-k-fold leakage, now corrected.

**H6 — Discriminant validity: VAAMR is developmental, not topical (provisional; surfaced by the pilot).** VAAMR stage is a direction in language that is *recoverable by direct supervision* yet *orthogonal to the content-similarity structure* of the embedding space — that is, it is not a topic taxonomy. *Confirmed by* a content-similarity classifier (a kNN / Correct-&-Smooth model that propagates labels along embedding similarity) performing at or near chance on held-out VAAMR, while a *directly-supervised* classifier on the **same** embeddings (the LLM consensus, or a linear probe) classifies well above chance — the stage signal is present in the features but is not aligned with what makes segments *similar* (topic, body region, affect). *Refuted by* a similarity-only model recovering VAAMR (which would show the label reduces to content clustering), or by direct supervision also failing (which would show the stage is not linguistically encoded at all). *Instrument:* the participant-grouped arm battery (`experiments/gnn_reliability/`; `graph_experiments.md`). *Pilot result: supported* — the content-similarity graph reaches grouped κ ≈ 0.05–0.14 vs the consensus while a probe and the LLM on the *same* features reach the human band and the human level respectively, and adding construct-definition similarity structure (CFiCS-style anchors) *lowered* reliability. H6 is the construct-validity reading of the very evidence that refutes H5 (Section 8.5): the property that defeats a similarity-based graph classifier is positive evidence that VAAMR indexes the developmental re-habituation trajectory (Section 2.2), not linguistic content.

Stating the hypotheses this way makes the pipeline's claims auditable: every hypothesis names the artifact that tests it, every test has a falsification condition, and the two-sided framing of H3a, H4, and H6 ensures that "the graph did not classify / VCE did not help / clinical convergence did not appear" is reported as a result rather than buried.

### 4.1 Stage 1 — Semantic Segmentation and Speaker Separation

The first stage divides the diarized sentence stream into semantically coherent participant *segments* — multi-sentence units of meaning that become the atomic unit of classification throughout the rest of the pipeline. The choice not to segment at speaker-turn boundaries embeds a theoretical commitment: the unit of analysis should be the semantically coherent utterance — the stretch of discourse that constitutes a single identifiable expression of an experiential state — rather than the conversational exchange unit. A participant may complete a coherent phenomenological report across multiple short turns interspersed with therapist back-channeling; conversely, a single long turn may contain both an experiential report and a shift to cognitive commentary. Segmentation at semantic rather than conversational boundaries produces units of consistent phenomenological coherence.

The algorithm computes sentence embeddings using a sentence-transformer model (Qwen/Qwen3-Embedding-8B by default; configurable), constructs a windowed cosine-similarity curve across the session (window size = 3), and identifies segment boundaries at local minima in the similarity curve adaptively thresholded to the session's own distribution (default: 25th percentile). This adaptive thresholding handles between-session variance: sessions with uniformly high semantic coherence require a lower absolute threshold than sessions with more variable content, but the algorithm applies the same percentile-based criterion across both. Boundaries are also accepted at temporal pauses exceeding a silence threshold (default: 1500 ms), and a minimum segment length (default: 30 words) prevents over-segmentation of dense dialogue. Segments exceeding a maximum word count (default: 200) are recursively split at their midpoint.

A foundational design decision concerns the handling of therapist speech. Therapist dialogue in MORE is heavily structured by PURER and has a systematically different linguistic register from participant speech: questioning forms, second-person address, clinically calibrated hedging. Including therapist segments in VAAMR classification would generate systematic false positives. The pipeline therefore separates participant and therapist segments at segmentation, submitting only participant segments to VAAMR classification while retaining therapist segments interleaved chronologically in the session timeline. This separation serves dual analytical purposes: therapists are excluded from VAAMR classification (preventing false positives in participant stage assignments), while their dialogue is separately classified against the PURER framework at Stage 3c (Section 4.8), forming the therapist-side complement to participant-side VAAMR that makes the dyadic cue-response analysis (Section 7.2) structurally complete.

### 4.2 Stage 2 — Construct Operationalization

Before classification, the VAAMR `ThemeFramework` is serialized to `theme_definitions.json`, preserving the full operational definition of each stage for researcher review and reproducibility. A *content validity test set* (`content_validity_test_set.jsonl`) is also exported, comprising the exemplar, subtle, and adversarial utterances from each stage definition tagged with their known correct labels. This set serves as the primary instrument for the content-validity assessment described in Section 5.1: the classifier can be run against it before processing real transcripts to verify that the LLM correctly handles both the easy cases (exemplars) and the difficult cases (adversarial utterances). When the classifier fails on adversarial items, the framework definitions or the prompt are refined before committing compute to the full corpus.

### 4.3 Stage 3 — VAAMR Classification

Each participant segment is submitted to multi-run LLM classification against the VAAMR framework. The LLM is presented with the segment text, a context preamble (the two to three preceding sentences in the session, capped at 300 words), and the full ThemeFramework definitions, and is asked to return a structured JSON classification with `primary_stage`, `primary_confidence`, `secondary_stage`, `secondary_confidence`, and a `justification` field that must cite specific language from the segment.

The justification field is analytically important beyond aggregation: it makes the LLM's inferential basis explicit and auditable. A justification that cites language not present in the segment, or that is formulaic rather than text-grounded, signals a classification that should be flagged for human review regardless of its confidence score. A `null` `primary_stage` is a valid response — it means the LLM judges the utterance to be irrelevant to the VAAMR framework (e.g., logistical small talk) — and constitutes an ABSTAIN ballot. To reduce position bias, the order in which the five stage definitions appear in the prompt is randomized between runs when temperature > 0.

Classification is repeated `n_runs` times per segment. The pipeline supports two distinct multi-run modes with consequentially different reliability semantics:

- **Multi-model runs** (`per_run_models`): each run uses a different LLM. Cross-model agreement provides the strongest reliability estimate available within the pipeline, since the runs are produced by independently trained models with different architectural and training-data properties.
- **Single-model stochastic runs**: the same model is called multiple times with temperature jitter. This produces a *stability* measure — agreement across stochastic samples from the same parameter distribution — which is a weaker form of evidence than cross-model agreement, since the runs share all trained parameters, biases, and definitional artifacts.

The current production configuration on Move-MORE Cohorts 1–2 uses single-model stochastic mode with `n_runs=3`. This yields a stability estimate, not independence-based reliability. Migration to multi-model classification is a near-term enhancement (Section 8.4); it is a configuration change rather than an architectural one.

For each segment, per-run ballots are aggregated via majority vote (`classification_tools/majority_vote.py`). The system distinguishes four agreement levels: `unanimous` (all raters agreed and no parse errors), `majority` (winning ballot count exceeds half but not all raters), `split` (no ballot reached majority), and `none` (all raters errored). Segments with `split` or `none` agreement are automatically flagged for human review (`needs_review=True`) and included in the priority human-coding queue. ABSTAIN counts as a valid ballot, not a parse error: a unanimous ABSTAIN result is a high-confidence judgment that the segment is unclassifiable, substantively different from a split between ABSTAIN and a stage label. When ties occur, CODED stages are preferred over ABSTAIN, and among tied coded stages, the one with higher mean confidence among its agreeing raters wins.

A confidence-tiering system integrates run consistency with per-run confidence into a single reliability indicator: *high* confidence requires both unanimous agreement across runs and per-run confidence above 0.80; *medium* requires majority agreement with confidence above 0.60; *low* covers everything else (including all split-vote segments regardless of confidence). The integration matters because high per-run confidence in a single run is not a reliable indicator of correctness — LLMs can be confidently wrong. The combination of consistency across runs and high per-run confidence is more robust than either alone.

### 4.4 Stage 3b — VCE Multi-Label Classification (optional)

When codebook classification is enabled, each participant segment is also submitted to multi-label coding against the 59-code VCE phenomenology codebook. Two complementary classifiers are run and their outputs reconciled by an ensemble.

The **embedding-based classifier** (`codebook/embedding_classifier.py`) encodes segments as *query* embeddings and codebook entries as *passage* embeddings using a retrieval-optimized sentence-transformer model. For each segment-by-code pair, a composite cosine-similarity score is computed weighted across the code's definition, inclusive criteria, and exemplar utterances. A *two-pass procedure* improves recall on subtle phenomenology: in the first pass, codes above a confidence threshold are identified; in the second pass, those high-confidence codes are merged into the exemplar pool and the segment is re-scored.

The **LLM classifier** presents the segment with the codebook definitions and elicits a multi-label classification with per-code justifications. Multi-label LLM coding uses a stricter consensus rule than single-label theme coding: a code is included only if a strict majority of raters apply it.

The **ensemble** (`codebook/ensemble.py`) reconciles the two classifier outputs. The current production behavior produces a *union* ensemble: codes assigned by either method above threshold are retained, with embedding scores weighted 0.6 and LLM scores 0.4 in the composite confidence. The master segment file additionally stores `codebook_labels_embedding` and `codebook_labels_llm` separately, so the *intersection* (codes assigned by both) is reconstructable as a stricter alternative. The default lift computation (Stage 4) operates on the ensemble (union) labels, which favors recall for exploratory phenomenological characterization. We will report both union-based and intersection-based lift tables in the empirical companion paper to this methodology, with the union table as primary for exploratory characterization and the intersection table as the validity-conservative complement.

### 4.5 Stage 4 — Cross-Framework Lift Statistics

When both VAAMR and VCE classifications have been computed, Stage 4 computes empirical lift statistics for each (VAAMR stage, VCE code) pair across the corpus:

> *Lift(stage, code) = P(code | stage) / P(code)*

A lift value above 1.0 indicates the code appears more often in segments classified as that stage than in segments overall; values above 1.5 we treat as substantively elevated. The computation includes a minimum count filter (default: 3 segments) to prevent a code that appears once in one stage from generating a spuriously high lift. The Stage 4 output is the corpus-level lift table and ranked associations above the 1.5 / 3-count thresholds.

As stated prominently in Section 3.3, Stage 4 currently computes empirical lift only — *not* an automated comparison against pre-specified expected codes. The `expected_codes` data structure that would enable mechanical expected-vs-observed comparison is the highest-priority engineering item for the between-cohort interval (Section 8.2).

### 4.6 Stage 5 — Validation Set Generation

Stage 5 generates a balanced human evaluation set by stratified sampling across stages and confidence tiers, ensuring the human validation sample represents the full distribution of classified content rather than over-sampling clear cases. The output (`human_coding_evaluation_set.csv`) is the formal blind-coding worksheet used for the inter-rater reliability protocol (Section 5.4), sized at approximately 10–20% of all labeled participant segments. Cross-session human-coding worksheets and AI-classification worksheets for matched comparisons are also produced.

The validation set is complemented by two other artifacts: the content-validity test set from Stage 2 (the known-label adversarial utterances) and the flagged-for-review list (segments with `split` or `none` LLM agreement). Together, these three artifacts provide three orthogonal entry points into human validation: known-label test of classifier behavior, flagged ambiguous cases that the classifier could not resolve, and stratified random sampling of confident classifications.

### 4.7 Stage 6 — Dataset Assembly

Stage 6 assembles the master dataset — every segment from every session, with its classifications, confidence tier, and final label resolved according to a strict priority hierarchy (frozen segments + classification overlays live in `qra.db`; the analysis layer reads the generated `master_segments.csv` export):

> adjudicated > human-consensus > gnn-consensus > LLM-zero-shot

At the start of a study, all final labels carry the source `llm_zero_shot`. As human coding is completed and entered, labels for coded segments upgrade to `human_consensus` (where a human coder independently assigned the same label as the LLM) or `adjudicated` (where a human expert resolved an LLM-human disagreement). The optional `gnn_consensus` tier (Section 8.5) sits between human and LLM and is engaged *only* when the graph-distilled consensus layer has passed its out-of-sample reliability gate and an analyst has set `gnn_layer.gnn_authoritative=True`; the raw LLM ballots are always preserved (`primary_stage`, `purer_primary`, `rater_votes`), so every graph override is auditable. This priority hierarchy makes the final-label provenance fully auditable: every claim about a segment can be traced to its source and the level of human verification it has received.

The chronologically-interleaved master file is itself the session adjacency index needed for cue-response analysis: full segment sequences per session sortable by `session_id` and `segment_index`, and the immediate temporal adjacency between any participant segment and the therapist contribution that precedes or follows it. A session-level stage progression summary (`session_stage_progression.csv`) reports counts of forward, backward, and lateral transitions between adjacent participant segments per session.

### 4.8 Stages 7 and 8 — Reports and Cue-Response Analysis

Stage 7 generates per-session coded transcripts (each segment with all classifications visible, rater ballots displayed, consensus decisions and confidence-tier annotations attached), human blind-coding worksheets, and per-session statistics aggregated to the cohort level.

Stage 8 — invoked by `python qra.py analyze` either at the end of a pipeline run or post-hoc on a completed run — generates the principal analytical reports: per-participant longitudinal trajectories; per-session deep analysis; per-theme corpora (every segment in the corpus assigned to each VAAMR stage, sorted by confidence); graph-ready CSVs; visualization figures of stage distributions, longitudinal trajectories, transition matrices, codebook lift heatmaps, and PURER fidelity distributions; and the cue-response analysis.

**Updated note on PURER classification.** Therapist segments are extracted, indexed in the chronological session timeline, and classified against the PURER framework in Stage 3c of the orchestrator. PURER classification of therapist segments is now operational on Cohorts 1–2 (as of late beta). The cue-response analysis therefore produces a *PURER move distribution* of therapist behavior at each transition type, answering the dyadic mechanism question at near-causal temporal adjacency. Human validation of PURER classifications is ongoing (target: Krippendorff's α ≥ 0.70) before the labels are used as primary evidence in curriculum modification recommendations.

The cue-response analysis is built on a simple but powerful observation about how group therapy actually unfolds. A participant speaks. The therapist responds. The participant speaks again. The empirical question is: what does the therapist do, exactly, when participants make a particular kind of stage transition?

For every within-session VAAMR transition — every participant segment at stage X immediately followed by a participant segment at stage Y — the analysis captures the tri-partite structure FROM → CUE → TO:

- **FROM**: the participant utterance preceding the transition, classified at stage X.
- **CUE**: all therapist segments whose `segment_index` falls strictly between the FROM and TO positions in the same session.
- **TO**: the participant utterance after the transition, classified at stage Y.

This is not merely the nearest therapist utterance; it is the full therapist contribution in the temporal gap between the two participant segments. The FROM → CUE → TO triple is the atomic unit of the cue-response analysis. Two reports synthesize the structure. The transition report documents the statistical landscape (counts of forward, backward, and lateral transitions; exemplar triples; per-participant trajectories). The cue-response report aggregates the full evidence per transition type: an LLM-synthesized characterization of the average FROM, average CUE, and average TO, drawn from every example of that transition type in the corpus, producing a compressed but interpretively rich portrait of what each kind of stage transition typically looks like — *with the caveat above that the CUE position contains unlabeled therapist text until PURER classification is implemented.*

Within-session and between-session transition matrices answer different questions. Within-session transitions capture the *texture* of a session: how fluidly a participant moves across stages, how often they access higher stages, what therapist cues are temporally associated with momentary advancement. Between-session transitions, computed from the modal stage of each (participant, session) pair, capture *learning*: whether the stage a participant primarily occupies advances week to week. A participant who reaches Reappraisal briefly within Session 2 but reverts to Vigilance as their dominant stage, then shows Reappraisal as their dominant stage by Session 5, has demonstrated the kind of consolidation across sessions that VAAMR predicts. The two levels together tell a richer story: within-session transitions show the therapist pulling the participant temporarily forward, and between-session transitions show whether those temporary advances have been internalized.

## 5. Validation: Text Psychometrics

The application of language model-based classification to phenomenological constructs requires a validation methodology appropriate to the distinctive epistemic situation: third-person computational tools applied to first-person experiential reports, where the ground truth is constitutively inaccessible to the classification system and only partially accessible to human coders. Standard psychometric frameworks designed for numerical scale scores do not translate directly to this context. Low, Mair, Nock, and Ghosh (2024) propose Text Psychometrics as a framework specifically designed for this purpose. QRA implements each component for the computational phenomenological context.

### 5.1 Content Validity: Known-Label Test Sets

Content validity in text psychometrics concerns whether the classification system adequately covers the construct domain. QRA's content-validity assessment is implemented through the known-label test set generated at Stage 2, which contains the exemplar, subtle, and adversarial utterances from each stage definition — utterances whose correct classification is fixed by the framework design itself. Running the classification pipeline against these known-label utterances *without* providing their labels yields a direct measure of content validity: per-stage accuracy on clear-tier items reveals how well the LLM has internalized the framework definitions; accuracy on adversarial-tier items reveals how well the pipeline handles conceptual boundary cases; systematic misclassification of specific adversarial utterances identifies the exact stage boundaries where the operational definition requires sharpening.

This assessment is conducted before processing real transcripts. Low accuracy on adversarial items — for example, systematic confusion between Avoidance and Vigilance on utterances combining avoidant intention with attentional capture — identifies a definitional boundary that needs clarification before classifications of real segments can be trusted. The assessment serves both as a validation check and as a prospective improvement instrument.

### 5.2 Construct Validity: Cross-Framework Lift, with Three Caveats

Construct validity concerns whether a measure correlates with other measures of theoretically related constructs. QRA's construct-validity assessment operates through the cross-framework lift statistics described in Section 4.5, evaluated against the theoretically motivated expectations stated in Section 3.3.

Three caveats need to be made before any construct-validity claim is taken seriously, two of which we have already raised and one of which we add here.

*First*, restated from Section 3.3: the expectations are stated in this paper, not encoded as a data structure in the codebase. Section 8.2 commits to remedying this before Cohort 3, populating `expected_codes` in `vaamr.py` and implementing the expected-vs-observed comparison in `cross_validation.py`. Until that commit lands, the construct-validity argument for Cohorts 1–2 rests on prose-level pre-specification rather than mechanical reproducibility.

*Second*, when both classifiers are LLMs trained on overlapping internet text, some portion of their co-occurrence will arise from shared training-data patterns rather than from genuine phenomenological structure in the transcripts. The terms "notice," "observe," and "aware" trigger both VAAMR Metacognition classification and VCE Meta-Cognition coding because both classifiers have learned the same lexical associations from the same training distribution. This is *construct overlap* operating at the level of classifier substrate, not mutual constraint operating at the level of independent measurement. A defensible test against this confound is the *shuffled-stage permutation control*: re-running the lift computation against a permuted assignment of segments to stages, breaking the real correspondence and recomputing the lift distribution. If the high-lift associations observed under the real assignment also appear under permutation, the apparent convergence is classifier co-dependency, not framework validation. We commit to this analysis as part of the same engineering item that adds `expected_codes` (Section 8.2); it is computationally trivial since it requires no new classifications.

*Third*, the union ensemble currently used as input to the lift computation favors recall and inflates co-occurrence relative to a stricter intersection criterion. A complementary lift table computed from intersection-only ensemble labels is reconstructable from the stored fields and serves as a conservatism check.

With these caveats made, the substantive interpretation of cross-validation results depends on what is observed: predicted (stage, code) pairs at lift ≥ 1.5 are consistent with the phenomenological theory of dys-appearance and re-habituation (Section 2); unexpected high-lift associations are hypothesis-generating findings about features the theory did not anticipate; predicted associations that do not appear are equally informative — they may reveal that the Move-MORE population's expression diverges from theoretical prediction in identifiable ways, requiring framework adaptation.

A second, structurally different construct-validity instrument addresses the first caveat (classifier co-dependency) head-on. The cross-framework lift table asks whether two LLM classifiers *co-fire* on the same segments, which shared training-data lexicon can manufacture. The VCE-on-VAAMR contribution test (hypothesis H3a, Section 3.4; `gnn_layer/ablation.py:vce_vaamr_contribution`) asks a different question that co-firing cannot answer: does forcing a *single learned model* to also predict the 59 VCE codes reorganize its shared representation so that it classifies the five VAAMR stages *better on held-out segments*? Co-occurrence driven by shared lexicon does not, by itself, improve held-out VAAMR κ; an improvement (Δκ > 0) therefore constitutes convergent-validity evidence that is not reducible to classifier co-dependency, complementing the shuffled-stage permutation control. A null result (Δκ ≈ 0) is the conservative outcome and is reported as such. The two instruments together — permutation-controlled lift (does VCE *converge* with VAAMR beyond chance?) and the graph contribution test (does VCE *inform* VAAMR classification?) — are what license, or refute, the dual-framework architecture on evidence rather than assertion.

### 5.3 Reliability: Multi-Run Consensus and Cross-Model Agreement

Inter-rater reliability in classical psychometrics measures the consistency of judgments across independent raters. Computational classification introduces a stochastic analog with two distinct semantic interpretations that should not be conflated.

When multiple *independent models* produce the runs, cross-model agreement is the strongest reliability estimate available within the pipeline. The runs are genuinely independent in a sense recognizable from classical inter-rater design: they share neither parameters nor training distributions, and their convergence on the same classification is evidence that the classification is grounded in the segment's content rather than in any single model's idiosyncrasies.

When a *single model* with temperature jitter produces multiple runs, the agreement statistic measures *stability* — agreement across stochastic samples from the same parameter distribution. This is weaker evidence: the runs share all trained parameters, biases, and definitional artifacts, and their convergence reflects only that the model's posterior distribution over labels is sharply peaked. High stability does not imply correctness; a confidently wrong model is stably confidently wrong.

Both forms of agreement are recorded and reported in the master segment file. The current Move-MORE production configuration uses single-model stochastic mode, providing stability rather than independence-based reliability. We are explicit about this in our reporting and in the human-validation protocol below: with single-model multi-run, the formal reliability claim that the pipeline can support is internal stability, and human validation against expert judgment carries the primary burden of establishing classification correctness. Migration to multi-model classification is a near-term priority (Section 8.4).

A third reliability instrument applies specifically to the graph-distilled consensus layer (Section 8.5). Because that layer is a *student* of the multi-run LLM consensus, its trustworthiness is measured as **out-of-sample agreement with the consensus it was trained to reproduce**: a k-fold cross-validation in which each fold's labels are masked during training and predicted afterward, reported as Cohen's κ **broken out per VAAMR stage and per PURER move** (`06_gnn/validation.txt`). The per-class breakdown is not cosmetic — it is the safeguard against the characteristic failure mode of graph smoothing, in which a rare but clinically central stage (Reappraisal) is averaged toward its more common embedding-neighbors. An aggregate κ can look healthy while Reappraisal recall quietly collapses; the per-stage table makes that visible and gates the graph's promotion to authoritative labels. Agreement against the human-coded subset (κ\[graph, human] beside κ\[LLM, human]) is reported alongside as the independent-quality axis.

**A methodological correction (Cohorts 1–2 pilot).** This cross-validation must hold out *whole participants*, not random segments. Random k-fold over a transcript graph **leaks**: a held-out segment's temporal-chain and kNN neighbours include its own and same-participant segments whose labels are visible during training, so the model effectively sees the answer. On the pilot corpus this inflated the gate from a participant-grouped κ ≈ 0.05 to a random-fold κ ≈ 0.25 — a near-fivefold illusion. The production gate now holds out whole participants by default (`StratifiedGroupKFold`, `config.participant_grouped_cv`), and every reliability κ is reported with a participant-clustered bootstrap confidence interval because the per-cell n is small. The pilot's *reliability ceiling* is itself informative for interpreting all VAAMR-based claims: trained human coders agree on VAAMR at Krippendorff α ≈ 0.33–0.52 (moderate — a genuinely fuzzy ground truth), the multi-run LLM consensus already matches the human raters (Cohen κ = 0.537 vs the human consensus, i.e. at the human level), and ≈36% of human-coded participant items are "No code" — segments expressing no VAAMR stage. The last point has a direct measurement consequence: a classifier that cannot *abstain* is wrong on roughly a third of the human-axis items by construction, which is one reason the LLM (which can answer "no code") tracks human judgment better than any fixed-five-class model (Section 8.5).

### 5.4 Human Validation: Two-Team Blind Coding

Computational reliability measures are necessary but not sufficient for validating phenomenological classifications. The ultimate validation standard is agreement with human expert judgment.

The current state of human validation in the Move-MORE pilot is as follows. Four qualitative researchers are independently coding a 20% stratified sample of QRA's VAAMR classifications across Cohorts 1 and 2, working blind to the LLM labels. The sample is balanced across stages and confidence tiers, ensuring representation of both the easy classifications (high-confidence, prototypical exemplars) and the hard ones (low-confidence, boundary cases). Before coding real segments, each rater works through the content-validity test set without seeing labels — a procedure that simultaneously calibrates rater familiarity with VAAMR distinctions and surfaces definitional ambiguities specific to the Move-MORE context (most importantly, the kinesthetic vocabulary introduced by the movement integration that was not present in the original MORE corpus on which VAAMR was derived).

Agreement statistics will be interpreted as follows.

- **Agreement at or above 75% raw agreement and Krippendorff's α ≥ 0.60**: computational classifications can serve as primary evidence for curriculum-modification recommendations. High-confidence and medium-confidence classifications are retained as valid; low-confidence classifications are treated as directional indicators requiring researcher confirmation.
- **Agreement between 50% and 74%, or α between 0.40 and 0.59**: classifications provide directional evidence only. Curriculum recommendations are framed as hypotheses to test in subsequent cohorts, not as conclusions, and all modification decisions require convergent evidence from at least two independent sources.
- **Agreement below 50% or α below 0.40**: classifications should not be used as primary evidence. The most likely explanations are that the VAAMR framework is insufficiently specified for Move-MORE's movement-specific language register, that the LLM is not reliably distinguishing between adjacent stages, or that participant utterances are more mixed-stage than the framework anticipated. In any of these cases, both rater teams review flagged segments and discuss definitional ambiguities, leading to framework refinement before Cohort 3 begins.

**Observed human↔human reliability (Cohorts 1–2, current state).** On the three transcribed VAAMR test-sets, agreement among the researchers is *moderate* and currently lands in the middle "directional evidence" band rather than the primary-evidence band: Krippendorff's α ≈ 0.47–0.52 on the two larger test-sets (pairwise Cohen's κ ≈ 0.43–0.55), with the smaller secondary-coded test-set lower still (pairwise κ as low as ≈ 0.17). The human raters themselves do not yet clear the α ≥ 0.60 bar on VAAMR primary codes. This is the expected signature of a genuinely difficult construct — adjacent stages (Attention Regulation vs Metacognition; Metacognition vs Reappraisal) are the recurring sites of disagreement — and it carries two consequences the pipeline takes seriously. First, it is itself a finding that drives the framework-refinement loop (definitional clarification before Cohort 3). Second, and crucially for interpreting every machine statistic that follows, it sets the *ceiling*: no LLM or GNN substrate can be expected to agree with a human consensus more reliably than the humans agree among themselves, so "human-level" agreement here denotes *moderate* agreement, not near-perfect agreement.

The architecture is designed around complementarity: the LLM applies framework definitions consistently across thousands of segments without fatigue; the human reads carefully where careful reading is needed. The flagged-for-review queue, the stratified evaluation sample, and the content-validity test set provide three orthogonal channels by which human attention is directed where it is most consequential.

### 5.5 The Inter-Rater Reliability Module (`qra irr`)

The reliability instruments of Sections 5.3–5.4 are operationalized in a single, re-runnable analysis: `qra irr`. Once the qualitative team's blind coding of the frozen validation worksheets has been transcribed into a wide CSV (one row per worksheet item; each researcher's primary and secondary VAAMR code, the human consensus of record, and the reasoning behind it), `qra irr import` normalizes the free-text labels to VAAMR ids, resolves each worksheet item to its frozen `segment_id`, validates the recorded consensus against a recomputed strict-majority rule (warning on any divergence), and persists the human codes into the project's `qra.db`. They are then **maintained as ground truth for all subsequent validation**: because the human codes live in the database and the machine labels are pulled live at analysis time, every later re-classification (new or additional LLM models) or graph retraining is re-measured against the same human anchor without re-importing.

**All chance-corrected statistics are computed with established, third-party libraries rather than bespoke implementations**, so the numbers are auditable against their canonical definitions: Cohen's κ via scikit-learn (`sklearn.metrics.cohen_kappa_score`; Cohen, 1960), Fleiss' κ via statsmodels (`statsmodels.stats.inter_rater.fleiss_kappa`; Fleiss, 1971), and Krippendorff's α via the `krippendorff` package (nominal level; Krippendorff, 2004). Because the test-sets contain missing ballots and unequal rater counts per item (Test-set 2 was split across two rater pairs), Krippendorff's α — which is defined natively for missing data — is the headline multi-rater statistic; Fleiss' κ is reported over complete-case items only (where every roster rater coded the item), and raw observed agreement accompanies both as a descriptive anchor.

The module computes three comparison families. **(1) Human ↔ Human**, per test-set, on both primary and secondary codes: pairwise Cohen's κ for every overlapping rater pair plus Fleiss' κ and Krippendorff's α across the roster — this is the irreducible reliability of the human coding itself, and it is the reference band every machine substrate must approach. **(2) Human consensus ↔ LLM**, against both the multi-run consensus *and each individual LLM rater/model* separately (recovered from the stored per-run ballots), with a confusion matrix and per-stage precision/recall. **(3) Human consensus ↔ GNN**, reported along *two distinct axes whose epistemic status is never conflated* (Section 5.3):

- The **held-out axis** uses the GNN's out-of-fold cross-validation predictions — each segment scored by a model trained with that segment's own VAAMR label masked. Held-out-GNN ↔ human is therefore an *independent* construct-validity estimate (the model never saw a label for that segment), and held-out-GNN ↔ LLM is the reliability gate of Section 5.3 restricted to the validation items. These are persisted during `qra gnn train` (`03_analysis_data/gnn/gnn_heldout_predictions.csv`).
- The **distillation axis** uses the in-sample consensus overlay — the GNN trained on *every* segment's LLM label, which is the operational default labeler. Distillation-GNN ↔ LLM is reported transparently as *distillation fidelity* and is understood to be optimistically high by construction (the model trained on the very label it is being scored against); it is shown for completeness and is explicitly **not** treated as validity evidence.

This dual reporting is the methodologically load-bearing choice. A naïve IRR that compared the production GNN to the LLM on the same segments it trained on would be circular and would inflate apparent agreement. By reporting the distillation axis as the operational default *and* the held-out axis as the honest validity measure side by side — against the human↔human and LLM↔human reference bands — the document makes the **gate decision** legible: the distillation GNN remains the default labeler until the *held-out* GNN's κ against human (and against LLM) is comparable to LLM-vs-human and human-vs-human, at which point the graph is eligible to label new segments LLM-free (Section 6.4; enforced by `qra gnn status`). Until then, showing both axes is the safeguard against mistaking a student's recall of its teacher for independent agreement.

Outputs are deliberately interpretable. Beyond the κ/α tables (`04_validation/irr/irr_results.json`, `irr_pairwise.csv`) and confusion-matrix and rater-agreement figures, the module emits a per-test-set, line-by-line dossier (`04_validation/irr/irr_items_testset_<n>.txt`) that lays out, for every item, the segment text alongside the human consensus and each rater's code *with their reasoning*, the LLM consensus and each model's code *with its justification*, the GNN held-out prediction, and the LLM↔GNN consensus — so that a disagreement is never just a number but a readable case. A ranked discrepancy walkthrough (`irr_discrepancies.csv` and the report's discrepancy section) surfaces exactly the items where the human consensus diverges from the machine substrates, directing expert attention where it is most consequential. The full report is `06_reports/06b_irr_report.txt`. When a project contains imported human codes, the IRR suite is **regenerated automatically during `qra analyze`** whenever the machine state (LLM or GNN labels, or the held-out predictions) has changed since the last run, so the reliability evidence never silently goes stale relative to the models it describes.

In practice on Cohorts 1–2 the LLM consensus reaches Cohen's κ ≈ 0.54 against the human consensus — squarely within the human↔human band reported in Section 5.4 — so the LLM achieves *human-level* reliability in the precise, bounded sense established there: it matches a moderately-agreeing human panel rather than exceeding it. Finally, because the entire comparison presupposes that the segments being scored are byte-identical to the text the humans actually coded, `qra irr run` re-verifies each frozen item's content hash before computing agreement and **refuses** (absent `--allow-drift`) if any segment has drifted since the worksheet was frozen — the failure mode a re-segmentation could otherwise introduce silently. The same content hash is folded into the change-gate, so `qra analyze` regenerates and prominently flags any drift rather than scoring stale text. This makes test-set↔content consistency an enforced invariant rather than a manual assumption.

## 6. The Move-MORE Feasibility Trial as the Methodology's Primary Pilot

### 6.1 The Trial in Brief

Move-MORE is a multicomponent, virtually-delivered mindfulness-and-movement program for adults with lumbosacral radicular pain (LRP) and elevated kinesiophobia (Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia ≥ 23). The intervention integrates three components grounded in Self-Determination Theory: mindfulness training adapted from MORE, gentle therapist-guided movement therapy targeting kinesiophobia within pain-tolerable ranges, and motivational coaching using motivational-interviewing-consistent techniques. Group delivery satisfies the relatedness need; individualized home-practice prescriptions support autonomy and competence.

The feasibility trial enrolls 32 adults (n ≈ 8 per cohort) across four sequential cohorts in an iterative convergent design (Alwashmi et al., 2019). Each cohort completes the eight-week MORE-for-Pain protocol with movement integration: Session 1 (What is Pain and Why Can Mindfulness Help?), Session 2 (Automaticity in Chronic Pain), Session 3 (Mindful Reappraisal), Session 4 (Mindful Savoring), Session 5 (Pain and Unhealthy Coping Mechanisms), Session 6 (Disrupting the Stress-Pain Link), Session 7 (Pain, Thought Suppression, and Mindfulness to Meaning), Session 8 (Maintaining a Mindfulness Practice). Each session follows a standard structure anchored by PURER-guided practice debriefing. The full protocol (Wexler et al., in preparation) is registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT07125027).

The qualitative-analysis component of the protocol — the component QRA implements — was originally specified as fine-tuning a BERT-base classifier on MORE codebook labels and generating session-level code-frequency heat maps for joint-display review. QRA preserves this overall workflow but extends it in three significant ways: (a) the underlying classification is performed by zero-shot multi-run LLM consensus rather than supervised fine-tuning, allowing the pipeline to run before a fine-tuning corpus exists; (b) the dual-framework architecture adds VCE multi-label coding and cross-framework lift statistics; (c) the cue-response infrastructure makes the dyadic structure of session interaction empirically interrogable in a way that session-aggregate code frequencies cannot.

### 6.2 Current State of the Pilot

As of completion of Cohort 2:

- **VAAMR participant-side classification** is operating across all completed sessions, with confidence tiering and multi-run consensus producing labeled segments at high, medium, and low confidence.
- **VCE 59-code phenomenology classification** is operating via the embedding-plus-LLM ensemble.
- **Cross-validation lift statistics** have been computed as exploratory characterization. The theoretically motivated expectations stated in Section 3.3 are being evaluated against the observed lift table for Cohorts 1–2 in the empirical companion paper to this methodology.
- **Within-session and between-session transition analysis** is producing transition matrices, exemplar (FROM, CUE, TO) triples, longitudinal trajectories per participant, and the cue-response report aggregating therapist language by transition type.
- **Human validation infrastructure** has been generated; four qualitative researchers are coding a 20% stratified sample.
- **Therapist segments** are extracted, indexed in the chronological session timeline, and classified against the PURER framework at Stage 3c. PURER classification of Cohorts 1–2 is operational (late beta); cue-response analysis now produces PURER move distributions at each transition type. Human validation of PURER labels is underway (Section 8.1).

The Cohort 1–2 corpus, once human-validated, becomes the methodology's first systematically labeled corpus of VAAMR stage expression in mindfulness-based pain therapy. It serves three purposes: empirical evidence base for Cohort 3 curriculum modification; convergent-evidence base for the dual-framework architecture as a methodological contribution; and initial training signal for supervised fine-tuning extensions (Section 8.4).

### 6.3 The Curriculum Modification Workflow

The curriculum modification report for Cohort 3 will be organized as follows. We describe it explicitly because translating pipeline outputs into curriculum decisions is the practical test of whether the methodology can do what it claims.

**Stage Distribution Summary.** Group-level prevalence of each VAAMR stage across Cohorts 1 and 2; the feasibility rating computed from segment-tier distribution; validation-agreement statistics. This section makes explicit what has been human-validated versus what is LLM-classified, and reports the longitudinal trajectory at the group level: does the group's mean stage progression slope upward across the eight sessions? Does it do so monotonically, or are there session-specific dips?

**Session-by-Session Analysis.** For each session, the observed stage distribution, notable deviations from theoretical expectation, and one to two representative participant quotes drawn from the highest-confidence exemplar segments. The theoretical expectation for each session is provided by the protocol structure: Session 3 (Mindful Reappraisal) should produce more Reappraisal-stage utterances than Session 1; Session 5 (Pain and Unhealthy Coping) is content-designed to catalyze the Avoidance → Attention Regulation → Metacognition progression; and so on. Where the empirical distribution deviates from theoretical expectation, that deviation is the hypothesis-generating observation for curriculum modification.

**Avoidance Barrier Assessment.** *Important framing: this section currently requires manual synthesis from the general transition report and the cue-response report; an automated `avoidance_barrier_report` that computes Avoidance prevalence by session number, Avoidance → Attention Regulation transition rate within and between sessions, and per-participant barrier-crossing classification (defined as the first session in which Avoidance is no longer the participant's dominant stage) is a near-term engineering target identified through the present manuscript's preparation (Section 8.2).* Once the automated report exists, this section reports the Avoidance prevalence and barrier-crossing patterns directly. Until then, the curriculum-modification team produces this section by running the existing transition report and reading the cue-response output for Avoidance → Attention Regulation transitions specifically. The Avoidance barrier is the single most clinically important dimension of the analysis: it is the central developmental challenge identified by Wexler, Balsamo et al. (2026), and the cue-response analysis directly addresses what therapists are saying when participants do or do not cross it.

**Therapist Behavior Analysis.** With PURER classification now operational, the cue-response report characterizes which therapist inquiry moves (Phenomenology, Utilization, Reframing, Education/Expectancy, Reinforcement) dominated the intervening dialogue for every observed VAAMR transition type — supporting therapist-fidelity assessment alongside curriculum recommendations.

**Recommended Modifications.** Each recommendation takes the form: *(Observation) + (Mechanism Hypothesis) + (Proposed Change) + (How to Assess Effect in Cohorts 3–4)*. For example: *Session 5 shows persistent Avoidance dominance and few Avoidance → Metacognition transitions despite content explicitly designed to catalyze that transition (observation). The therapist cue patterns at the few observed transitions emphasize Phenomenology inquiry and de-emphasize Reframing (mechanism hypothesis). Adding explicit Reframing prompts at the practice debrief in Session 5 may increase the transition rate (proposed change). Effect can be assessed by comparing Cohort 3's Session 5 stage distribution and transition rate against Cohorts 1 and 2 (assessment plan).* This format converts pipeline outputs into actionable, falsifiable curriculum decisions and supports the MoSCoW (Must, Should, Could, Would-Like) prioritization specified in the Move-MORE protocol.

**Validation Caveats.** The agreement statistics from the human validation, with explicit identification of which recommendations are supported by validated, high-confidence classifications and which rest on directional computational evidence requiring Cohort 3 confirmation. This section is not boilerplate. It is the place where the epistemic limits of the methodology are explicitly attached to specific recommendations, so that the curriculum team can weight them appropriately.

### 6.4 From Human Coding to Graph Scaling: The Validation Progression Across Four Cohorts

The methodology is not a static pipeline applied uniformly to every cohort. It is a deliberately staged progression in which the *authority* of each classification layer is earned, and the analytic substrate shifts as evidence accumulates — from human judgment, to human-grounded LLM consensus, to a graph distilled from that consensus. The four-cohort iterative design of Move-MORE is the timeline along which this progression unfolds, and understanding it is essential to understanding why the pipeline is built the way it is.

**Stage 0 — Human coding of the original MORE corpus is the root of authority.** The VAAMR framework was not invented for this pipeline; it was derived by Wexler, Balsamo et al. (2026) from line-by-line human qualitative coding of MORE-for-LRP therapy transcripts, and the operational definitions, prototypical features, and exemplar/subtle/adversarial utterances that the LLM classifier reads in its prompts are the distillate of that human coding work. Every downstream label in this pipeline ultimately traces its construct definitions to human phenomenological coding of real therapy dialogue. This is the bedrock: the LLM is not asked to invent a classification scheme, it is asked to apply a *human-derived* one consistently at scale.

**Stage 1 — Cohorts 1–2: LLM consensus is validated against human coding until it reaches inter-rater reliability.** In the first two cohorts, the production substrate is zero-shot multi-run LLM consensus (Section 4.3). This consensus is not trusted on its own authority. A 20% stratified sample of its VAAMR classifications (and, in parallel, its PURER classifications) is blind-coded by four qualitative researchers, and the agreement between the human consensus and the LLM consensus is quantified as raw agreement and Krippendorff's α against the targets of Section 5.4 (α ≥ 0.60 for VAAMR, ≥ 0.70 for PURER). The human-coded subset is simultaneously the *validation instrument* and a *bootstrap signal*: the cases humans adjudicate become the highest-authority labels of record (`adjudicated` / `human_consensus`, ranked above any machine label in Section 4.7's provenance hierarchy), and they anchor the LLM consensus to human judgment precisely where the classification is hardest. The deliverable of Cohorts 1–2 is therefore not just a labeled corpus but a *measured warrant*: a quantitative statement of how far the LLM consensus can be trusted as a proxy for expert human coding, stage by stage.

**Stage 2 — Cohorts 3–4: the graph is developed for rapid, low-cost analysis once it reproduces the consensus to reliability.** The cost structure of Stage 1 — multiple frontier-LLM calls per segment, plus a human validation pass — does not scale to a growing corpus, and it is the bottleneck the original Move-MORE protocol anticipated when it specified a fine-tuned classifier as the eventual analysis engine (Section 6.1). The graph-distilled consensus classifier (Section 8.5) is the realization of that intent. During Cohorts 1–2 the GNN trains on the full multi-run LLM ballot distribution — itself bootstrapped by the human-coded subset — and learns to reproduce the LLM majority-vote consensus from graph structure (semantic-similarity and temporal-chain edges over Qwen3 embeddings). Its trustworthiness is then measured exactly as the LLM's was: out-of-sample, per VAAMR stage and per PURER move, as Cohen's κ against the consensus it was trained to reproduce *and* against the human-coded subset (`06_gnn/validation.txt`, Section 5.3). Only when that gate clears `irr_target` with no rare-stage collapse — and only when an analyst explicitly promotes it — does the graph become eligible to classify *new* Cohort 3–4 segments with no LLM calls at all (`qra gnn classify`). The default remains LLM classification; the graph earns its authority the same way the LLM did, by demonstrated agreement against human-grounded labels. This is what makes "graph development for rapid analysis in Cohorts 3–4" a *validation-gated* acceleration rather than a leap of faith: the expensive, human-validated Stage-1 procedure manufactures the training signal and the held-out yardstick that license the cheap Stage-2 procedure.

**Pilot revision (Cohorts 1–2).** The first real-corpus gate refutes the graph-scaling step *in its classifier role* at this scale (hypothesis H5; Section 8.5): under leakage-free participant-grouped cross-validation the graph does not reproduce the consensus to reliability (grouped κ ≈ 0.05–0.14), and a linear probe on the same Qwen features does not close the gap. The *logic* of the progression is unchanged and, in fact, vindicated — the held-out, participant-grouped yardstick is precisely the instrument that exposed the shortfall (and the random-fold leakage that had masked it). What is revised is the Stage-2 *substrate*: the LLM consensus — already at the human level and affordable at trial scale — remains the labeling and analysis-scaling engine through Cohorts 3–4, and the graph is carried forward as a **mechanism and discovery** instrument rather than a label producer. Whether *any* learned classifier (a graph, or the fine-tuned model of Section 8.4) can earn the scaler role is re-opened at the larger N of the later cohorts, since the binding constraint here is data scale (n≈32), not the design of the gate. A dedicated distillation campaign (§8.6) subsequently established the current best realization of that scaler role — a per-rater ensemble at classifier↔human κ 0.450 / classifier↔LLM κ 0.361, which dominates the single probe but does not yet clear the equivalence bar — confirming the constraint is data scale and positioning the model as an assistive, *gated* pre-labeler until N grows.

The progression is therefore monotone in earned authority and monotone in scale: human coding defines the constructs, LLM consensus applies them across the full corpus under continuous human audit, and the graph — once it has proven it reproduces the human-grounded consensus to reliability — carries the analysis forward at trial speed for the cohorts where speed matters most. Each layer remains visible beneath the next (raw LLM ballots are never discarded, human adjudication always outranks machine labels), so the progression adds capability without ever erasing the evidence trail that justifies it.

### 6.5 Patient and Public Involvement

The Move-MORE protocol incorporates patient partners as co-investigators and a Community Advisory Board. The QRA workflow is designed to support this involvement rather than bypass it. The reports the pipeline produces are human-readable: coded transcripts with classification justifications visible per segment, session-level summaries with prototypical exemplars, longitudinal trajectories per participant. Patient partners can read the reports, identify cases that match or diverge from their own experience, and contribute to MoSCoW prioritization. The pipeline accelerates and structures the analysis; it does not replace the deliberative process by which modification decisions are made.

## 7. What This Methodology Uniquely Reveals

The defensibility of any new method rests on what it makes knowable that was not knowable before. We articulate five contributions that QRA makes possible in MBI research that are not achievable through conventional qualitative methods, conventional process research, or single-framework computational classification alone.

### 7.1 Per-Utterance Phenomenological Staging at Full-Trial Corpus Scale

Wexler, Balsamo et al. (2026) identified the VAAMR stages through thematic analysis of thirty MORE sessions. That work was painstaking and methodologically careful, but it could not produce a per-utterance phenomenological classification of every participant utterance in every session of a full clinical trial. QRA does. Every participant segment in every session of Move-MORE is assigned a VAAMR stage label with associated confidence, justification, and (optionally) multi-label phenomenological codes. This density of labeling, at this resolution, is simply not achievable by human coders within the temporal constraints of iterative trial design.

What this enables is not merely more data but a different kind of analysis. Stage distributions per session become tractable. Per-participant longitudinal trajectories become tractable. Within-session transition matrices, between-session dominant-stage transition matrices, and progression-score regressions across sessions become tractable. The empirical texture of therapeutic progression — its variability across participants, its session-specific patterns, its responsiveness to curriculum changes — becomes visible at a resolution at which it was previously invisible.

### 7.2 Mechanism Evidence at Temporal Adjacency, Not Session-Level Correlation

Most psychotherapy process research either codes therapist behavior or codes client outcomes and correlates the two at the session or treatment-episode level. *In sessions where therapists used more Phenomenology inquiry, did participants score higher on decentering at session end?* This is correlational evidence at session aggregation. It is informative but coarse, and it is vulnerable to confounding by participant characteristics, alliance quality, and a host of other session-level variables.

QRA's cue-response analysis operates at temporal adjacency, not session aggregation. The question becomes: *In the participant utterance immediately following this specific therapist contribution, what stage appears, relative to the stage of the participant utterance immediately before?* The unit of analysis is the FROM → CUE → TO triple. The therapist's contribution is bracketed exactly; the before and after are immediate. This is much closer to mechanistic evidence than session-level correlation can provide. It cannot establish causality (it remains naturalistic observation), but it controls for participant-level and session-level confounders by virtue of operating at the within-participant, within-session, within-minutes level.

**Updated mechanism evidence claim.** PURER classification is now operational (as of late beta). The CUE position in FROM → CUE → TO triples is now labeled with PURER move type (Phenomenology, Utilization, Reframing, Education/Expectancy, Reinforcement). The pipeline can now answer questions of the form *what does the therapist do, exactly, when participants cross the Avoidance barrier?* — by retrieving every observed Avoidance → Attention Regulation transition in the corpus and computing the distribution of PURER moves in those CUE positions. The synthesis is now a quantified PURER-move distribution, answered at a resolution that conventional process research cannot match. Human validation of PURER classifications (target: Krippendorff's α ≥ 0.70) is underway to ensure reliability before these move distributions are used as primary evidence in mechanism claims.

### 7.3 Both Sides of the Therapeutic Dyad from the Same Input

In conventional process research, therapist behavior and client experience are typically coded separately, often by different teams using different frameworks, with the resulting analyses correlated post hoc. QRA derives both labels from the same diarized transcript, in the same pipeline pass, using the same architectural assumptions. The internal consistency of dyadic analysis is structural rather than achieved through reconciliation. With PURER classification now operational, the dyadic structure is fully labeled: every participant segment has a VAAMR stage; every therapist segment has a PURER move; every transition has a (PURER move composition, VAAMR transition type) pair. The PURER × VAAMR lift table is the empirical test of the PURER facilitation hypothesis at the resolution where it actually operates.

### 7.4 Empirical Evaluation of Cross-Framework Convergent Expectations

The theoretical predictions stated in Section 3.3 derive from the phenomenological theory of dys-appearance and re-habituation articulated in Section 2 and from the contemplative-tradition empirical record encoded in VCE. The cross-validation lift statistics provide empirical evaluation of these predictions on Cohorts 1–2.

The framing matters. Per the disclosures of Sections 3.3 and 5.2, the current cross-validation does not mechanically test pre-specified hypotheses encoded in the codebase; it generates an empirical lift table that can be evaluated against expectations stated in this manuscript. Patterns observed in Cohorts 1–2 generate refined and code-level-specifiable expectations that — once `expected_codes` is populated in `vaamr.py` (Section 8.2) — become the formal pre-registered hypotheses tested by Cohorts 3–4 as replication. *This staged structure is what scaffolds the conditions for a Varela-style mutual-constraints test*: the iterative cohort design provides the replication architecture, and the engineering commitment to pre-specification before Cohort 3 makes the predictions falsifiable from the codebase rather than from the prose alone.

This kind of cross-framework empirical test was not previously tractable in MBI research. It is possible here because the same corpus is classified against both frameworks computationally, allowing the lift table to be computed at corpus scale.

### 7.5 A Validated Labeled Corpus as Cumulative Trial Output

A frequently overlooked feature of computationally-augmented qualitative analysis is that the labeled corpus produced as its byproduct is a durable research asset. The Cohort 1–2 corpus, once human-validated, supports supervised fine-tuning of a domain-adapted classifier (Section 8.4). Extended by Cohorts 3 and 4, it will, by trial end, constitute the largest systematically labeled corpus of VAAMR stage expression in any mindfulness-based pain intervention. This is a contribution to MBI process research that persists beyond the trial — a resource for future validation work, replication studies, and cross-trial comparisons.

### 7.6 Context-Dependent Therapist Effects: A Central Scientific Hypothesis

The bilateral architecture of QRA — classifying both participant and therapist language against independent phenomenological frameworks — enables a specific and falsifiable empirical hypothesis that is central to the Move-MORE research program but has not previously been tractable to test at corpus scale.

> **Hypothesis:** The same informational content delivered by a therapist can produce systematically different participant VAAMR stage outcomes when delivered through different PURER move types, and the directionality of this effect depends on the participant's current stage.

This hypothesis rests on the co-occurrence structure documented in `theme_framework/purer.py`. Consider a therapist informing a participant that mind-wandering is normal during meditation. When this content is delivered as **Education** (E) — *"The mind wanders; that's how attention works"* — it provides conceptual scaffolding, which theory predicts is most effective for Vigilance-stage participants who lack a framework for understanding their failure to sustain attention. When the same content is delivered as **Reframing** (R) — *"What you're describing — the mind drifting — that IS the practice"* — it anchors the concept to the participant's specific narrative, which the VAAMR model predicts is more effective for Avoidance-stage participants (by recasting their "failure" as "practice") and for Metacognition-stage participants (by modeling the act of observing mind-wandering as mindfulness itself). The critical prediction is that the participant's *from_stage* moderates the relationship between the PURER move type and the *to_stage*.

QRA can test this hypothesis empirically because the FROM → CUE → TO triple preserves all three elements simultaneously:

1. **FROM** — the participant's VAAMR stage before the therapist contribution
2. **CUE** — the therapist's PURER move type (with precedence rules resolving co-occurrence)
3. **TO** — the participant's VAAMR stage following the therapist contribution

The `purer_analysis.py` module computes the full conditional lift table: for each (from_stage, to_stage) pair, what is the distribution of dominant PURER moves in the mediated cue blocks? This is the `conditional_table` output in `02_mechanism/purer.txt` (Section 2). The test of the hypothesis is whether, for semantically similar therapist content, different PURER move types produce different conditional-probability profiles across to_stage values, and whether those profiles differ as a function of from_stage.

Two examples illustrate the clinical significance of this analysis. First, when a participant at Avoidance describes using breathing to escape pain, a therapist could respond with **Education** (E) — explaining the hurt-versus-harm distinction — or with **Reframing** (R) — identifying the reported experience as avoidance. The hypothesis predicts that E produces a higher probability of Attention Regulation (the participant understands the concept and applies it) while R produces a higher probability of Metacognition (the participant observes their own avoidance pattern). Second, a **Utilization** (U) move — *"How will you use this tomorrow?"* — delivered to a Vigilance-stage participant may produce frustration (lateral or backward transition) because the participant has not yet established the attentional stability required to deploy skills forward, while the same U delivered to a Metacognition-stage participant is predicted to produce forward transitions toward Reappraisal because the participant can successfully imagine deploying the observed insight.

The corpus-level aggregation in `purer_transition_profiles.csv` provides the empirical evidence to evaluate these directional predictions. The pipeline does not establish causation, but the within-participant, within-session temporal adjacency controls for participant-level confounders in a way that session-level correlation cannot. The hypothesis is explicitly falsifiable: if no systematic differentiation in to_stage distributions emerges as a function of PURER move type conditional on from_stage — that is, if the conditional profile table shows uniform distributions across PURER constructs for semantically overlapping therapist content — then the operational distinction between PURER moves carries less clinical significance than the MORE protocol implies. Conversely, if the predicted profile differentiation emerges, this constitutes convergent empirical evidence that the PURER taxonomy captures clinically meaningful variation in therapeutic technique, and that technique matters in ways that are stage-specific rather than content-driven.

### 8.4 Multi-Model Reliability Migration and Supervised Fine-Tuning (Priority 4, concurrent with Cohorts 3–4)

Two related upgrades to the classification infrastructure.

First, migrating the production configuration from single-model stochastic mode (`n_runs=3` with one model and temperature jitter) to multi-model mode (`n_runs=3` with three different LLMs, e.g., one frontier closed model and two diverse open-weights models) converts the reliability semantics of the pipeline from stability to genuine cross-rater agreement. This is a configuration change rather than an architectural one. The migration can be staged on a subset of segments first to compare cross-model agreement to single-model stability and characterize where the two diverge.

Second, the Cohort 1–2 validated corpus, formatted as labeled examples for sequence classification, supports fine-tuning a domain-adapted transformer classifier. The advantages over zero-shot LLM classification are reduced inference cost, improved boundary-case accuracy on the corpus's own boundary cases (including human-corrected misclassifications), and deployability for ongoing therapeutic-fidelity monitoring at a scale and cost that zero-shot does not support. The graph-distilled consensus layer (Section 8.5) is an *already-implemented* realization of this scale-and-cost extension: it learns from the LLM-and-human-labeled corpus and, once it passes its out-of-sample reliability gate, classifies new transcript segments with no LLM calls at all (`qra gnn classify`, or the "Classify — Graph consensus" action in the TUI). The fine-tuned transformer remains the heavier-weight alternative for cross-trial deployment. The fine-tuning workflow can be orchestrated using an LLM-driven hyperparameter-search controller that repeatedly modifies a training script and scores the result against a fixed held-out validation split. The epistemological requirements for fine-tuned deployment in *new* populations are more demanding than for the current exploratory use within Move-MORE: validation against independent samples — ideally from MORE trials not included in Move-MORE training data — would be required before fine-tuned deployment could be claimed to have the same validity basis as the human-expert-grounded zero-shot approach.

### 8.5 Graph-Neural-Network Representation and Discovery Layer: Implemented

**Status: implemented; repositioned after the Cohorts 1–2 pilot.** The discovery/mechanism layer runs by default; the GraphSAGE *consensus-distillation classifier* is now a separate, **default-OFF** subpackage (`gnn_layer/classifier/`, `gnn_classifier_enabled=False`) because the pilot refuted its scaler role (the repositioning paragraph below is load-bearing — read it before the original design intent in this paragraph). The GNN layer (`gnn_layer/`) is a pure-PyTorch GraphSAGE analysis layer that runs at analyze-time (after `master_segments` assembly), gated by `config.gnn_layer.enabled` (ON by default; opt out with `enabled=False`) or `qra analyze --gnn`. It plays two roles. First, it is a *discovery and triangulation* layer that augments the LLM-label pipeline without replacing any classifier, targeting three structural limits the LLM approach cannot close: (a) superposition is discarded at majority vote, (b) therapist cues are collapsed to five labels before influence is measured, and (c) both convergence measures are LLM-on-LLM. Second — and this is the consequential extension over the prior design — it is a *consensus-distillation classifier*: it learns to reproduce the multi-run LLM majority-vote consensus (which is itself bootstrapped by the human-validated subset) from graph structure, and once it reproduces that consensus to inter-rater reliability *on held-out segments* it can label new transcript segments with no LLM calls and — at the analyst's explicit choice — become the authoritative label of record. *(The pilot below **refutes this scaler role at Cohorts 1–2 scale**, which is why the classifier now ships default-OFF; the paragraph is retained to document the original design intent and the `gnn_authoritative` promotion path that remains in the code for re-adjudication at larger n.)*

**Pilot finding and repositioning (Cohorts 1–2, n≈32; full record in `graph_experiments.md` and `design_decisions.md`).** A pre-registered arm battery on the first real-corpus pilot, evaluated under **participant-grouped** cross-validation (whole participants held out — the only leakage-free protocol; §5.3), repositions this layer. Three results are decisive. *(i) The consensus-distillation classifier / scaler role (hypothesis H5) is not supported at this scale.* With real Qwen3-Embedding-8B features the graph reproduces the LLM consensus on held-out **participants** at κ ≈ 0.05–0.14 — far below the κ ≥ 0.70 gate and below even the human↔human band (§5.4). An earlier κ ≈ 0.25 that appeared closer to passing was an artifact of **random k-fold leakage**: in a transcript graph, a held-out segment's temporal and kNN neighbours include its own and same-participant segments whose labels are visible, so random folds let the model see the answer. The honest, participant-grouped gate is now the default (§5.3). *(ii) A linear probe on the same Qwen features ties or beats the graph* (human κ ≈ 0.37 probe vs ≈ 0.36 graph; LLM-axis κ 0.31 probe vs 0.21 graph), and pure graph smoothing (Correct-&-Smooth on the probe) is the **worst** arm. This is the diagnostic signature that **VAAMR is not homophilous in the embedding space**: VAAMR is a *developmental* state, but cosine similarity tracks *content* (topic, body region, affect), so similarity edges systematically connect different-stage segments and two rounds of message-passing blur the very distinction being classified. Adding CFiCS-style construct-definition anchor nodes *lowered* reliability further, for the same reason — the homogeneous, content-similarity graph cannot recover a process label. *(iii) The two levers that did improve reliability were not graph machinery* but measurement discipline and construct operationalization: class-weighting (which recovered the rare stages from 0% held-out recall) and an explicit **"No code" null category** (≈36% of participant segments express no VAAMR stage — logistics, greetings, off-topic talk — and only a model that can abstain matches the human coders there). **The position we adopt on this evidence:** the multi-run LLM consensus remains the label of record and the analysis-scaling engine — it is already human-level (κ = 0.537 vs human, §5.4) and affordable at trial scale — and the graph is **dropped as a classifier of record** and retained as a **mechanism and discovery instrument** (Tracks B–D below), where its graph-native readouts, not its reproduction of labels, are the contribution. The reliability gate is kept as the honest, grouped, per-stage yardstick; `gnn_authoritative` promotion remains available in the code but, on this evidence, is not exercised. *In the codebase this repositioning is now structural:* the GraphSAGE consensus-distillation classifier and its gate live in a separate subpackage (`gnn_layer/classifier/`) that is **OFF by default** (`gnn_classifier_enabled=False`; enabled explicitly with `qra gnn train` to re-adjudicate at Cohorts 3–4 scale), while the discovery and mechanism instruments below — H6 discriminant validity, the dyadic transition model, confound localization, subtext communities — run by default on the raw embeddings, independent of any trained classifier.

**A discriminant-validity corollary.** The graph's *inability* to classify VAAMR from semantic similarity is not only a negative engineering result; it is positive evidence about the construct. A label that a content-similarity graph could recover would be, in effect, a topic taxonomy. VAAMR is not recoverable that way — which is consistent with the framework's claim that the stages index a *developmental re-habituation trajectory* (attentional stance toward the lived body, §2.2) that is orthogonal to what a participant happens to be talking about. We record this as a standing, testable observation (provisionally H6): the same property that defeats the graph classifier is evidence that VAAMR captures something beyond linguistic content.

**Why distillation rather than a consensus ballot.** An earlier design considered injecting the GNN as one additional rater in the per-segment majority vote. That is circular: the GNN's stage head is trained on the very LLM ballots it would then vote beside, so its vote double-counts the LLM and inflates apparent agreement — precisely the LLM-on-LLM convergence the methodology treats as weak evidence (Sections 5.2–5.3). The distillation design avoids this: the graph does not *add a vote*, it *replaces the aggregator*, integrating the full per-rater ballot distribution with relational structure that per-segment voting discards — semantically similar segments should receive similar labels (kNN edges), a participant's stage does not oscillate randomly across the developmental arc (temporal-chain edges), and the full ballot mixture is preserved rather than collapsed to argmax. Its added value over majority vote is therefore measurable as the cases it resolves that majority vote could not (split and low-confidence segments) and as its agreement with *human* judgment.

**The reliability gate and the over-smoothing safeguard.** Because the graph could in principle smooth a rare class toward its neighbors, its authority is gated by measurement rather than by a hand-tuned override rule. A k-fold cross-validation masks each fold's labels during training and predicts them afterward; agreement with the LLM consensus is reported *per VAAMR stage and per PURER move* (`06_gnn/validation.txt`, Section 5.3). The graph is recommended for LLM-free scaling only when overall κ clears `irr_target` (default 0.70, matching the human-validation targets) *and* no rare stage — Reappraisal above all — has collapsed in held-out recall. Promotion to authoritative labels is never automatic: the default flow stays LLM-classification, the analyst reviews the per-stage gate, and only then sets `gnn_layer.gnn_authoritative=True` (or accepts the TUI's recommendation). The raw LLM ballots remain visible per segment throughout, so the human-validation protocol (Section 5.4) can code blind once and compare against *both* the raw LLM majority and the graph consensus.

**Architecture.** The layer builds a heterogeneous graph over Qwen3-Embedding-8B vectors (reused from segmentation and VCE, no additional model). Node types: `participant_segment`, `therapist_segment`, and optional construct anchors (VAAMR, PURER, VCE). Edge families: temporal chain (FROM→CUE→TO structure in graph form), anchor/label edges weighted by classification confidence, kNN similarity edges for inductive attachment of new sessions, and optional cross-framework edges weighted by empirical lift. GraphSAGE message-passing is implemented in pure PyTorch (`gnn_layer/model.py`) — no torch-geometric required. Multi-task heads: soft-VAAMR (KL to ballot mixture), progression-coordinate regression, VCE multi-label BCE, PURER CE, supervised-contrastive InfoNCE over the ordered VAAMR arc, and self-supervised link-prediction on the temporal chain.

**Five Capabilities — all directional until human validation catches up.**

*Capability A — Continuous superposition.* Per-segment VAAMR stage-mixture vectors and continuous progression coordinates from GNN geometry. These are the primary mixture source when enabled (`SuperpositionConfig.mixture_source='gnn'`), feeding all downstream mechanism and efficacy inference with partial-progression resolution the argmax cannot express. Output: `03_analysis_data/gnn/segment_positions.csv`.

*Capability B — Cue motif discovery (flagship).* Therapist cue blocks are mean-pooled into GNN embeddings and clustered into emergent motifs — patterns in therapist language that cut across the five PURER categories. Each motif is scored for influence on forward VAAMR transitions (from-stage-conditioned logistic regression, preserving the §7.6 stage-moderation hypothesis), labeled for PURER purity, and flagged if influential but poorly explained by existing labels. These flagged motifs are *candidate new therapeutic constructs* surfaced with exemplar cues for human review — the deepest realization of the curriculum-modification mission. Outputs: `cue_motifs.csv`, `cue_block_assignments.csv`, `06_gnn/emergent_motifs.txt`.

*Capability C — Independent measurement substrate.* GNN-derived VAAMR stage assignments compared against LLM-ensemble and human-coded labels via Cohen's κ (`06_gnn/triangulation.txt`). GNN-lift tables for VAAMR×VCE and PURER×VAAMR placed beside LLM-derived tables — GNN↔LLM convergence is stronger construct-validity evidence than LLM↔LLM, directly complementing the planned shuffled-stage permutation control (§8.2). Outputs: `gnn_vs_llm_lift.csv`.

*Capability D — Construct signal ablation and the VCE-on-VAAMR test (optional).* Two related instruments adjudicate whether the granular VCE codebook earns its place (hypothesis H3a, Section 3.4). The *head ablation* (`run_gnn_ablation=True`) removes each construct head (VCE, PURER) and retrains; the training-loss delta is a directional ranking of how much independent signal each family carries (*is VCE superfluous?*). The more decisive instrument is the *VCE-on-VAAMR contribution test* (`test_vce_layer=True`, `gnn_layer/ablation.py:vce_vaamr_contribution`): on the identical graph, folds, and seed it runs the held-out reliability cross-validation twice — with the `vce_multilabel` head active and with it removed — and reports the change in out-of-sample VAAMR κ vs the LLM consensus, overall and per stage (`06_gnn/vce_contribution.txt`, `gnn_vce_contribution.csv`). Δκ > 0 is direct evidence the fine-grained phenomenology layer sharpens the coarse five-stage classification; Δκ ≤ 0 is the empirical warrant to retire VCE from the classifier of record. Consistent with this methodology's position that VCE earns its place only insofar as it demonstrably helps, **VCE graph anchor nodes are excluded by default** (`include_vce_nodes=False`); a study that wishes to test the hypothesis turns the head on and runs these instruments. Outputs: `06_gnn/vce_contribution.txt`, `construct_signal.txt`, `gnn_vce_contribution.csv`, `gnn_construct_signal.csv`.

*Capability E — Participant↔therapist coupling.* Latent NMF/PCA factors of therapist cue language and their correlation with subsequent participant VAAMR forward movement. Factors are named post-hoc against an inline CF/IC alliance-concept reference lexicon — alliance-like structure is *discovered*, not imposed. Exemplar quotes and forward-correlation values are surfaced for human interpretation. Outputs: `coupling_factors.csv`, `06_gnn/coupling.txt`.

**Hardening the LLM-free classifier (Track A).** Distillation fidelity is necessary but not sufficient to let the graph label of record at scale: a confident-wrong label on a genuinely-new segment would poison the corpus the next cohort trains on. Five opt-in, individually-*measured* mechanisms close that gap, each kept only if it earns its place on the held-out gate. (A1) Typed therapist→participant *precipitates* edges let the participant representation attend to the preceding cue, gated by a learnable per-edge-type weight that initializes to the identity so the default graph is unchanged until an ablation shows the family raises κ on both the LLM and human axes. (A2) Per-VAAMR-stage **abstention/deferral** lets the graph decline a low-confidence segment — the LLM label is then kept rather than a graph guess promoted — with the floors optionally calibrated to a target held-out precision (rare/hard stages naturally demand more confidence). (A3) **Temperature scaling** on the held-out logits plus an **out-of-distribution** score (mean cosine distance to the training support) force abstention on genuinely-new transcripts that fall outside the graph's experience, rather than over-trusting in-distribution softmax. (A4) Optional post-training **label propagation** diffuses the model's soft predictions over the graph, retained only if it raises held-out κ by a measured margin. (A5) A **scale-mode simulation gate** holds out whole sessions, attaches them inductively (kNN-only, the real scaling condition), and flags domain-shift risk when inductive κ falls materially below the in-sample gate κ. All five are off by default; the homogeneous, fixed-weight graph is the conservative baseline, and each mechanism must demonstrate it helps before it is enabled. Reports: `06_gnn/{precipitates_contribution,label_propagation,scale_sim}.txt`; the gate verdict (including the fitted temperature) is persisted to `gnn_gate.json`.

**Track B — the dyadic FROM→CUE→TO transition model (mechanism instrument; rebuilt).** The *observed* Δprogression of cue blocks — the signed change in the participant's progression coordinate from the turn before a therapist cue to the turn after — is the direct, label-of-record evidence for "what therapist language progresses participants," analyzed with full inference in `analysis/mechanism.py` (participant-clustered bootstrap CIs, within-stratum permutation, FDR, mixed-effects; §7.2, H2). The original GNN lens read a *per-segment classifier's* incidental sensitivity to a swapped therapist feature; on the pilot that *inverted* the observed ranking (Spearman ρ = −0.13) and was mis-specified for mechanism three ways (`GNN_MASTER_PLAN.md` §4.7: kNN content-noise on a *process* question; never trained on transitions; the cue reaching a participant node through a single diluted edge, ≈0.03 shift). It has been **replaced** by a small dyadic **transition model** (`gnn_layer/transition.py`) trained directly on the FROM→CUE→TO triples — `TO_mixture ≈ f(FROM_mixture, FROM_stage, pooled raw-Qwen cue)`, with **no kNN** (H6: similarity neighbours are stage-mixed) and FROM-stage conditioning baked in (the partial control for the elicitation confound — moves are compared *within* a starting state). It is validated by an **earns-its-place** participant-grouped CV (does the cue improve held-out TO prediction over a FROM-only baseline?), and its counterfactual then reads a genuine **learned response function** (swap the cue to each PURER centroid → predicted shift in the following participant's E[stage], per move and per from-stage × move, participant-clustered bootstrap CIs), **triangulated** against `mechanism.py`'s observed ranking. A companion **confound-localization** instrument (`gnn_layer/confound.py`) maps the signed divergence (observed − learned counterfactual) per (from_stage × move) — where the elicitation/responsiveness confound most distorts the observational signal. This is **sensitivity analysis of a model, not causation** (n≈32 observational + the confound, §9.2/§9.4). **Pilot result (n≈32):** the cue does *not* improve held-out TO prediction (the transition is under-identified, consistent with H2's pilot status) — so the counterfactual is exploratory and `mechanism.py` leads — yet the learned counterfactual triangulates *positively* with the observed ranking (Spearman ρ ≈ +0.34, versus the prior classifier-counterfactual's −0.13), directional support for the rebuild that the larger cohorts can adjudicate. Outputs: `06_gnn/{transition_model,confound_localization}.txt`, `03_analysis_data/gnn/{transition_counterfactual,transition_per_move,confound_localization}.csv`.

**Track C — the MindfulBERT training-set builder.** The end goal of the GNN work is not the GNN; it is a training dataset to fine-tune *MindfulBERT* — a domain-adapted model that does not merely classify VAAMR but predicts which language patterns progress a participant across VAAMR stages (§8.4). `process/assembly/mindfulbert_dataset.py` assembles that dataset over cue blocks: each example pairs the preceding participant context and VAAMR state with the therapist cue language, labelled by the **observed Δprogression** (signed coordinate change + a categorical direction). Every example carries per-example provenance and confidence — the *weakest* of its two endpoints' label-source tiers (adjudicated > human-consensus > gnn-consensus > LLM), an abstention flag, and the gate verdict — so the dataset never claims more certainty than its weakest label. An optional **transition-counterfactual augmentation channel** (the transition model's "would-progress" estimate, `gnn_layer/transition.py` → `transition_per_move.csv`) is added as a *separate, provenance-tagged* signal — sourced from the mechanism instrument (not the retired classifier) and gated on it having run — and it is **retained only if** a held-out, participant-grouped ablation shows it improves a lightweight progression-prediction proxy; at n≈32, where the cue is under-identified, it typically does not and is stripped from the exported examples. A datasheet records the provenance mix, gate status, the augmentation-ablation result, and the n≈32/observational caveats. This realizes the §8.4 fine-tuning intent as a *validation-gated* data product: the observed transition leads, the model-counterfactual signal only augments-under-validation, and a model's guesses are never distilled into another model's training set un-gated. Outputs: `02_meta/training_data/mindfulbert_dataset.jsonl`, `mindfulbert_datasheet.{json,txt}`.

**Track D — subtext communities as routines.** Motifs (Capability B) and coupling factors (Capability E) describe single therapist moves and latent cue dimensions; the deeper qualitative question is which language *routines* flow together and recur across sessions. `gnn_layer/communities.py` builds a thresholded (cosine ≥ τ) cross-session segment-similarity graph — distinct from the trained kNN graph; it links segments that say the same kind of thing anywhere in the corpus — and partitions it with **two independent algorithms** (Louvain modularity optimization and agglomerative hierarchical clustering), reporting their adjusted Rand index so a community counts as structure rather than an algorithm artifact. Within-session community→community transitions model routines ("routine X tends to precede routine Y"). Because raw communities are fragile at n≈32, **participant-bootstrap stability selection** resamples participants, re-detects communities, and measures each community's co-membership stability; communities below the stability floor are *suppressed from the findings and flagged*, not silently dropped (citing the stability-selection / consensus-clustering literature, not CFiCS, which has no community detection). Survivors are named with TF-IDF terms and exemplar quotes (plus an *atypical* exemplar — the member farthest from the community centroid — for human close reading), with per-session prevalence and cross-cohort distribution as drift hypotheses, and each is described by its VAAMR-stage distribution and the mean observed Δprogression of the cue blocks it appears in. The deepened layer adds **dyadic routines** — therapist-community(CUE) → following participant-community(TO) transitions ("this kind of therapist language tends to precede this kind of participant language"), reported only between stability-selected communities and carrying observed Δprogression with participant-clustered CIs. Because H6 (the discriminant-validity instrument below) shows these communities are organised by *content*, not stage, the routines are content leads for human reading, not effects. The layer is gate-independent discovery and entirely hypothesis-generating. On the pilot the subtext graph needs a lower cosine threshold for instruction-tuned (Qwen) embeddings (τ≈0.6: a probe showed τ=0.85 yields only noise, Louvain↔hierarchical ARI≈0.003, versus ARI≈0.29 at τ≈0.6). Outputs: `06_gnn/{communities,dyadic_routines}.txt`, `subtext_communities.csv`, `subtext_community_transitions.csv`, `dyadic_routines.csv`.

**The H6 discriminant-validity instrument.** The discriminant-validity corollary above is operationalized as a reproducible instrument (`gnn_layer/discriminant.py`, default ON): on identical participant-grouped folds and the *same* Qwen embeddings it scores a supervised probe (above chance) against a content-similarity model (Correct-&-Smooth, ≈ chance) on both the LLM and human axes with participant-clustered CIs and a paired Δκ contrast, then characterizes the geometry — how much of the stage signal the leading *content* principal components recover, the local cosine-kNN stage homophily (weak and uneven: a probe decodes the stage that similarity neighbours do not share), and the community×stage ARI (≈ 0, independent). It packages the H5 refutation as the positive H6 finding and bounds every similarity-based method (Track D included) and the kNN edges the transition model drops. Output: `06_gnn/discriminant_validity.txt`.

**Epistemological position.** All GNN *discovery* outputs (motifs, coupling factors) are directional/hypothesis-generating until human validation catches up (§5.4); any emergent motif or coupling factor used in curriculum modification must pass human review (the same content-validity / blind-coding discipline as VAAMR and PURER) before it becomes primary evidence (§5, §9.5). The *consensus-distillation* outputs do not override the labels of record until two conditions are met: the graph passes its out-of-sample, per-stage reliability gate (§5.3), and an analyst explicitly promotes it (`gnn_authoritative=True`); even then, human-adjudicated and human-consensus labels still rank above the graph (§4.7), and the raw LLM ballots remain visible for audit. The distillation-fidelity reading (Capability C, GNN-vs-LLM) is by construction expected to be high; the load-bearing validity evidence is the GNN-vs-human axis and the held-out gate. The `human` and `self_supervised` label modes remain available for studies that want the graph trained off a non-LLM signal entirely. The same discipline governs the newer layers: Track-B's transition-model counterfactual is *model sensitivity, not causation* and is reported as primary only where the cue *earns its place* under participant-grouped CV and converges with the observed Δprogression (at pilot scale it does neither, so `mechanism.py` leads and the counterfactual serves as an exploratory lens and a confound localizer); Track-C augmentation is retained only under its held-out ablation and never merged with the observed labels (no model-distilling-model un-validated); Track-D communities below their stability floor are flagged, never reported as findings; the H6 discriminant-validity instrument is construct evidence, not a classifier. See `docs/GNN_MASTER_PLAN.md` for the full design record (the locked decisions, the defensibility spine, the per-track specifications, and the configuration reference), and `design_decisions.md` (§10) + `graph_experiments.md` for the executed record (the per-arm ledger, the honest negatives, and the discovery/mechanism rebuild).

### 8.6 Bootstrapping a Scalable LLM-Free Classifier from the LLM Consensus: The Distillation Campaign

**Motivation.** §8.5 settled the *architecture* question — a linear probe on Qwen features, not the graph, is the defensible cheap VAAMR classifier — but left the *scaling* question open: can such a probe be bootstrapped, from the human- and LLM-labeled examples already in hand, to a fidelity high enough to label new cohorts and the bulk MindfulBERT corpus without per-segment frontier-LLM cost? We ran a pre-registered distillation campaign to answer this on the Cohort 1–2 corpus (n≈32; full record in `src/experiments/classification_scaler/` — `CAMPAIGN_LOG.md`, `RESULTS.md` — with the cross-campaign catalog and the experimental-refinement workflow in `src/experiments/CATALOG.md` and `WORKFLOW.md`), holding the validated apparatus from §5.3 fixed: participant-grouped `StratifiedGroupKFold` folds, a dual-axis scorer (classifier↔LLM-consensus on the 205 labeled segments **and** classifier↔human on the 66-item validation subset, both via Cohen's κ), and participant-clustered bootstrap confidence intervals — each arm tuned on the LLM axis with the human items read once. The success bar was set at the construct's own ceiling: classifier↔LLM grouped κ ≥ 0.45 (the human↔human band, §5.4) **or** classifier↔human κ ≥ 0.50 (approaching the LLM's own human-level 0.537), CI-aware. Each experiment ran in an isolated git worktree and nothing was committed; the researcher promotes what the evidence warrants.

**The winner is a per-rater ensemble.** The campaign's best model improves on the single probe by declining to collapse the three checker LLMs into a majority vote *before* learning. The shipped consensus discards the raters' disagreement structure, which is itself signal; distilling **one class-weighted logistic probe per rater** (gemma-4-31b, nemotron-3-nano-30b, qwen3-next-80b — abstentions mapped to the "No code" null, errors dropped per rater) and ensembling them by mean predicted probability raises classifier↔LLM κ from the single probe's 0.283 to **0.361 [0.281, 0.432]** (a paired, participant-clustered Δ of **+0.078 [+0.036, +0.132]**, excluding zero) and classifier↔human κ from 0.365 to **0.450 [0.319, 0.599]** — dominating the single probe on both axes at no inference cost beyond three small linear models over cached embeddings. The result is corroborated convergently: an independent soft-label arm (an MLP fit to the full multi-run ballot mixture) lands on the same LLM-axis figure (κ 0.367), and an explicit "No code" two-stage decoupling lands on the same human-axis figure (κ 0.447). Three unrelated methods, one frontier.

**No cheap classifier reaches LLM-equivalence at this scale, and the reason is data, not modelling.** Neither axis clears the bar at its point estimate (best LLM 0.361 < 0.45; best human 0.450 < 0.50, though its interval includes it), and the convergence of three independent methods on classifier↔LLM ≈ 0.36 / classifier↔human ≈ 0.45 is the signature of a ceiling rather than an unfound trick. Every lever beyond the winner was tested and either tied or hurt, each reported with intervals: model capacity (MLP, gradient boosting, and RBF-SVM all 0.13–0.23, below the linear baseline), confidence calibration, and ordinal-arc decoding (which collapsed predictions onto the endpoints). Most diagnostically, **context** — the natural explanation for the LLM's residual edge, since it classifies each segment with its preceding turns in the prompt while the probe sees the segment in isolation — *failed to transfer*: concatenating an embedding of the six preceding turns to the target embedding *lowered* κ in both the weak (MiniLM, 0.158→0.125) and strong (Qwen-8B, 0.283→0.227) embedding spaces, and embedding the combined text collapsed it (κ ≈ 0.04). The LLM's contextual advantage is therefore its *reasoning over* context, not the mere presence of context in a feature vector — which a linear student cannot cheaply distil. Stacking the two winning levers (per-rater ensemble × the two-stage No-code gate) also hurt, because splitting the 134 No-code examples three ways starves each per-rater gate. The single binding constraint across every arm is the two rare stages, Avoidance and Metacognition, whose held-out recall stays near 0.28–0.35 regardless of method: the ceiling is the small number of labeled participants carrying those stages, compounded by the shallow separability of a developmental label in a content-trained embedding — the same H6 property (§8.5) that defeats the graph.

**Position adopted.** We therefore ship the per-rater ensemble as an **assistive, gated, abstention-aware pre-labeler — not an autonomous replacement for the LLM consensus.** This is the concrete realization of the validation-gated acceleration §6.4 anticipates: the model fills segments the LLM has not labeled, abstains (to "No code") below a calibrated confidence floor, and writes to a `probe_consensus` provenance tier ranked *below* `llm_zero_shot`, never overriding it; a per-project gate must clear the human band on that project's own human subset before the model is permitted to scale at all. At pilot scale this tier is rarely engaged — the multi-run LLM is affordable and already human-level — so its payoff is prospective (the bulk MindfulBERT corpus and later cohorts), and because the ceiling is *data*, its fidelity is expected to rise as labeled participants accumulate, re-gated at each cohort. The full implementation specification — the `qra probe train/status/classify` surface, the per-rater ensemble model, the overlay and provenance wiring, and the corresponding removal of the GNN's unexercised classifier-of-record surface — is recorded in `scalable_classification_master_plan.md`.

## 9. Epistemological Limits and Honest Caveats

The methodology's contributions need to be paired with honest acknowledgment of what it cannot do. We articulate five structural limits that follow from the kind of inference QRA performs and that no engineering improvement can dissolve.

**9.1 Linguistic expression is not phenomenological state.** QRA classifies what participants *say*, not what they *experience*. Participants in a group therapy setting are simultaneously experiencing pain, attending to the therapist's questions, monitoring other group members, managing social impression, and trying to find language for processes that may not have readily available linguistic representations. The VAAMR stage expressed in a participant's words at any given moment reflects all of these factors — not only the underlying phenomenological state. This limit applies equally to human qualitative coders and to LLM classifiers; it is structural to the interpretation of language as evidence of experience. The advantage of the LLM approach is consistency at scale; the disadvantage is the inability to draw on the contextual, relational, and clinical knowledge an experienced researcher brings to a difficult classification. The methodological response is human validation, designed not as a corrective applied after the fact but as integral to the analytical process.

**9.2 Naturalistic adjacency is not causal mechanism.** The cue-response analysis provides temporal-adjacency evidence of associations between therapist contributions and subsequent participant stage expressions. It is much closer to mechanistic evidence than session-level correlation can be. But it is not causal evidence. It is naturalistic observation in an unblinded, uncontrolled, multi-confounded setting. Many other variables — participant readiness, prior session content, group dynamics, alliance quality — influence what stage a participant expresses next. The temporal adjacency controls some of these confounders but not all. The appropriate epistemic stance is: the cue-response analysis provides a systematic, corpus-level description of the empirical relationship between therapist behavior and participant stage outcomes. It is evidence to be interpreted by clinical experts, not a prescription to be followed mechanically.

**9.3 Frameworks derived elsewhere constrain application here.** Both phenomenological frameworks were derived in contexts that differ from Move-MORE. VAAMR was derived from MORE sessions for LRP without movement integration; the kinesthetic vocabulary that movement introduces may express VAAMR stages through different surface forms than the framework exemplars currently capture. VCE was derived from Western Buddhist meditators with substantial practice experience; many VCE codes may appear rarely or not at all in eight weeks of clinical MBI practice. This is not a reason to abandon either framework. It is a reason to treat the cross-validation results as genuinely informative about fit. If theoretically predicted (stage, code) associations do not hold empirically in Move-MORE data, this is important evidence that the frameworks require contextual adaptation.

**9.4 Therapist influence on participant phenomenological expression.** PURER-guided phenomenological inquiry is specifically designed to direct participants' attention toward features of their experience that MORE considers therapeutically relevant. A Phenomenology question — *"What are you noticing right now in your body as you describe this?"* — elicits metacognitive-sounding language regardless of the participant's underlying experiential state, because it invites a metacognitive stance. VAAMR stage classifications therefore characterize the *dyadic expression* of therapeutic interactions, not purely participant phenomenological states. This limitation is a reason to pursue PURER classification (Section 8.1), not a reason to abandon VAAMR classification: with both participant and therapist segments classified, the pipeline can distinguish *participant-initiated* stage expression (Metacognition appearing in a participant segment without an immediately preceding Phenomenology inquiry) from *therapist-facilitated* stage expression. The former is stronger evidence of developmental attainment than the latter. **The pilot supplies direct empirical evidence that this confound is substantial, not hypothetical.** The dyadic transition model (Section 8.5, Track B) estimates a PURER move's influence *independent of when it is deployed*, and its confound-localization map reports, per (from_stage × move) cell, the signed divergence between that counterfactual and the observed Δprogression. On the pilot corpus nine of twenty cells *invert in sign* — observed and counterfactual point opposite ways — precisely the pattern expected when a genuinely-helpful move is nonetheless deployed *at moments of participant difficulty*, so its observed association with forward movement is depressed by the state that prompted it. (An earlier, mis-specified per-segment-classifier counterfactual inverted the *whole* ranking at ρ = −0.13; the rebuilt model's global rank correlation is positive, ρ ≈ +0.34, so the confound is now read at the cell level — a divergence map — rather than from a single inverted coefficient.) The observed associations of Section 7.6 therefore cannot be read as therapist *effects* without confronting this responsiveness selection; at the pilot's scale the two cannot be separated, which is the deepest reason the mechanism claims remain hypothesis-generating (H2).

**9.5 Computational phenomenology cannot replace phenomenological judgment.** The pipeline's output is hypotheses about phenomenological expression, generated by a system that has no access to first-person experience and that operates on the linguistic surface of what participants say. Human validation is not optional methodological fastidiousness; it is the epistemological requirement that distinguishes computational phenomenology from pattern-matching. The pipeline's contribution is to direct expert attention efficiently — to surface the cases where attention is most needed, to characterize patterns at scales where pattern characterization is intractable manually, and to produce reproducible empirical evidence about whether independently-derived phenomenological frameworks achieve coherent triangulation on the same experiential territory. It cannot substitute for the phenomenologically-trained clinical judgment that determines what those patterns mean and what should be done about them.

## 10. Conclusion

This paper has argued that chronic pain constitutes, in Leder's phenomenological terms, a dys-appearance of the lived body, and that mindfulness-based therapy for chronic pain is most coherently understood as structured contemplative re-habituation — a systematic practice for restoring functional structural relationships between attention and the lived body. The Vigilance-Avoidance-Attention Regulation-Metacognition-Reappraisal (VAAMR) model, derived by Wexler, Balsamo et al. (2026), describes the trajectory of this re-habituation in phenomenological terms grounded in participant first-person reports.

The Qualitative Research Algorithm represents an attempt to operationalize the empirical study of contemplative transformation at the scale and tempo that iterative clinical trial design requires. By implementing two independently-derived phenomenological frameworks as classification targets for LLM analysis, by examining their empirical convergence through lift statistics evaluated against theoretically motivated expectations, and by capturing the dyadic structure of session interaction at temporal adjacency, QRA *scaffolds the conditions for* a Varela-style (1996) mutual-constraints test that the four-cohort iterative design enables: Cohorts 1–2 generate empirical patterns under theoretically motivated description; Cohorts 3–4, preceded by code-level pre-specification of `expected_codes` in `vaamr.py` before they begin, formally test whether those patterns replicate.

The contribution we claim is modest but defensible. The pipeline cannot characterize phenomenological states directly; it cannot validate whether linguistic expressions of contemplative transformation correspond to genuine experiential attainments; it cannot substitute for expert phenomenological judgment. What it can do is make systematic phenomenological analysis of full clinical-trial corpora tractable within the temporal constraints of iterative design, make the dyadic structure of therapist-participant interaction empirically interrogable at the resolution where mechanism actually operates, and direct expert attention to precisely the cases where that attention is most needed. These are not small achievements. They are what the temporal mismatch between phenomenological understanding and iterative trial development has previously prevented.

What the methodology uniquely reveals — the per-utterance phenomenological staging at corpus scale, the temporally-adjacent mechanism evidence, the dyadic structure derived from the same input, the empirical evaluation of cross-framework convergent expectations, the cumulative validated corpus produced as byproduct — is not achievable through any one of the conventional alternatives. It is the integration of computational tractability with phenomenological seriousness that makes the integration valuable, not the computation alone.
